# Covariate Shift & the Closed-Loop Gap

> *Covariate Shift & the Closed-Loop Gap — why behavior cloning "drifts further the longer it drives, until it avalanches," and how industry pins it down with three cameras, synthetic perturbation, and closed-loop training.*

## 1. Terminology & Definition

### 1.1 Covariate Shift / Input Distribution Shift

**Covariate shift**, also called **input distribution shift**, means:

$$
p_{\text{train}}(x) \neq p_{\text{test}}(x), \quad \text{while the conditional } p(y \mid x) \text{ stays the same}
$$

The input distribution seen at training differs from that at deployment, but "given the input, what the correct answer is" is unchanged. No matter how well the model fits the training distribution, once deployment inputs fall into regions rare/unseen in training, predictions become unreliable.

### 1.2 Distinguishing Neighboring Concepts

| Shift type | What changes | Driving example |
|---|---|---|
| **Covariate shift** | input marginal $p(x)$ changes, $p(y\mid x)$ fixed | trained on sunny city, deployed in rainy-night suburbs |
| Label / prior shift | label marginal $p(y)$ changes | training set is 90% "go straight," deployed in a curve-dense area |
| Concept drift | $p(y\mid x)$ itself changes over time | traffic rules changed, lane lines repainted |

This note discusses only the first. The key point: **in imitation learning / closed-loop control, this shift in $p(x)$ is not imposed externally — the policy creates it itself.**

## 2. Two Layers: Incidental in General ML vs Inevitable in IL/Closed-Loop

### 2.1 In General Supervised Learning: External, Incidental

Ordinary ML assumes i.i.d. data. Covariate shift here is **external and incidental** — sampling bias, regional/seasonal change, sensor swaps. The dataset is **fixed**; the model does not change what it will see in the future. Remedies are "passive compensation" like importance weighting and domain adaptation.

### 2.2 In IL / Closed-Loop Control: Internal, Structurally Inevitable

Once an imitation-learned policy **runs in a closed loop on the car**, things change qualitatively: the policy's **current output determines what state it sees next**. So the policy induces **its own state-visitation distribution** $d_\pi$, which **must differ** from the expert's $d_{\pi^*}$:

$$
d_\pi \neq d_{\pi^*}
$$

This is the **fundamental difference between closed loop (policy affects its own input distribution) and open loop (dataset is fixed)**. In ordinary supervised learning covariate shift "may happen"; in an IL closed loop it "must happen" — as long as the policy is not bit-for-bit identical to the expert, it will take itself into states the expert never demonstrated.

### 2.3 Error Bound (Cited, Not Re-Derived)

The [Imitation Learning](../../02_Robot_Learning/02_Methods/模仿学习.en.md) note already gives the classic Ross & Bagnell result: if behavior cloning errs with probability $\epsilon$ per step, the total loss over a $T$-step trajectory is bounded by $J(\pi_\theta)-J(\pi^*)\le O(\epsilon T^2)$ (**quadratic** growth), while DAgger reduces it to $O(\epsilon T)$ (linear) by querying the expert on states the policy itself visits. This note does not re-derive it; it uses the result to explain driving.

### 2.4 Why "Even a Tiny ε Is Fatal in Driving"

The quadratic bound is only a statistical expectation. In autonomous driving three amplifiers make even a tiny per-step $\epsilon$ potentially fatal:

- **Nonlinear absorbing states → positive-feedback avalanche**: vehicle dynamics make a small deviation not a "linearly accumulating error" but a push toward out-of-training-distribution "absorbing regions" (crossing lines, mounting the shoulder, swerving into oncoming traffic). Once in a state the policy never saw, the output is near-random → drifts further → state even more unfamiliar — a positive feedback that drives the error **exponentially** off the expert manifold (see Figure 1).
- **Asymmetric cost of safety**: supervised loss weights all errors equally (0.5 m off and 2 m off have similar MSE), but the real-world cost is **step-like** — 0.5 m off is still in-lane, 2 m off is out-of-bounds/collision. So "small expected error $\epsilon$" badly **underestimates tail risk**.
- **Time scale**: the $T$ in $\epsilon T^2$ corresponds to large physical displacement at highway speeds; for long-horizon tasks $T$ is far from negligible.

## 3. Open-Loop vs Closed-Loop Evaluation: the Gap

The most insidious consequence of covariate shift is that it makes **offline evaluation systematically overestimate** a model's true performance.

### 3.1 Open-Loop (Offline Replay) Evaluation

The model is placed on **expert-recorded trajectories**, and its predicted action is compared frame-by-frame against the expert's (metrics like L2 displacement error, action-classification accuracy). Note: state advancement is always driven by the **expert** — the model **never actually drives** — so it stays on $d_{\pi^*}$ forever and covariate shift never gets a chance to occur. The result is **systematic optimism**.

### 3.2 Closed-Loop (Sim/On-Road) Evaluation

The model **actually drives** the vehicle, and the next state evolves from **its own actions**. Only this exposes $d_\pi\neq d_{\pi^*}$ and error accumulation; metrics are success rate, collision rate, violation score, takeover count.

### 3.3 Why Good Offline Metrics ≠ Good On-Road

Codevilla et al. (2018) found empirically: **open-loop L2 error correlates only weakly — even negatively — with closed-loop driving success**. A model with lower open-loop L2 can have a worse on-road success rate. The reason is exactly that open-loop evaluation locks the model onto the expert distribution and cannot measure what happens after it leaves the distribution. **Selecting a model by offline metrics alone gets systematically misled by covariate shift.**

### 3.4 Closed-Loop Leaderboards

The only trustworthy yardstick for diagnosing covariate shift is **closed-loop evaluation**:

- **CARLA Leaderboard**: drive preset routes in the CARLA simulated city, scored by driving score (route completion × infraction penalty: red-light running, collision, line-crossing each deduct).
- **nuPlan**: the first large-scale **real-data** closed-loop planning benchmark, distinguishing non-reactive (background traffic replayed from log) from reactive (background traffic reacts to ego) closed loops, closer to real interaction.

### 4.1 DAgger and Its Driving Variants

The core idea is to query expert labels on **states the policy itself visits**, actively pulling the training distribution toward $d_\pi$ (framework and $O(\epsilon T)$ guarantee in [Imitation Learning](../../02_Robot_Learning/02_Methods/模仿学习.en.md)). The deployment difficulty in driving is that **online expert queries are expensive and dangerous** (a human driver must watch and correct in real time). Variants: **SafeDAgger** (query the expert only when the policy is uncertain, saving labels), **HG-DAgger** (human-in-the-loop interactive takeover correction), or a rule-based/privileged expert replacing the human.

### 4.2 NVIDIA Three Cameras / View Synthesis for Recovery

PilotNet mounts left/center/right cameras on the car: the center camera pairs with the expert steering as a normal sample, while the left/right biased cameras are paired with "should steer right/left to correct" labels — manufacturing many "deviate-and-recover" samples (further interpolated by geometric view synthesis). It approximates DAgger's effect **with data rather than online queries**, the simplest and most effective engineering trick (see Figure 3 left).

### 4.3 ChauffeurNet's Synthetic Perturbation + Auxiliary Loss

ChauffeurNet trains on a mid-to-mid representation with two key moves: ① during training **actively perturb the expert trajectory** (create off-road, offset, hard-braking), forcing the network to learn to recover from bad states; ② add **auxiliary losses** (collision, off-road, on-road, geometry consistency) to explicitly penalize dangerous states. It "rehearses disaster" in training, a further systematization of §4.2 (see Figure 3 right).

### 4.4 Noise Injection / DART

DART (Laskey et al., 2017) **injects noise into the expert's actions** while collecting demonstrations, so the expert naturally visits and demonstrates "deviate-and-recover," widening the state distribution the demonstrations cover. It approximates DAgger at collection time but **needs no online expert**, more deployable than DAgger.

### 4.5 Closed-Loop Training + RL Fine-Tuning

Let the policy roll out itself in a **simulated closed loop**, using RL/reward (or online DAgger) to directly correct out-of-distribution behavior — making "training distribution = deployment distribution" real. This corresponds to the "end-to-end RL" quadrant in the [paradigm taxonomy](../01_Foundations/自动驾驶范式与方法谱系.en.md) (e.g., Roach trains with an RL coach in the CARLA closed loop, then distills to an IL student).

### 4.6 Data Augmentation and Long-Tail Scenario Mining

Actively mine long-tail/takeover/disengagement scenarios for targeted data: shadow mode (run the model in the background of production cars, log when it disagrees with the human driver), hard-example reflow, adversarial scenario generation. Essentially targeted expansion of the high-risk tail of $d_\pi$.

### 4.7 World Models Generating the Long Tail

Use generative world models (GAIA-1 etc.) to **synthesize** rare dangerous scenarios, serving both as recovery training data and as a closed-loop evaluation environment, bypassing the risk and cost of collecting such scenarios in the real world. See [Representation & World Models](../../00_Robotics/01_Embodiment_Theory/表征与世界模型.en.md).

| Method | How it approximates $d_\pi$ | Needs online expert | Needs sim | Representative |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DAgger / SafeDAgger / HG-DAgger | query expert on policy-visited states | yes | no | Ross 2011 / driving variants |
| Three-camera view synthesis | spatial bias makes recovery data | no | no | PilotNet |
| Trajectory perturbation + aux loss | temporal perturbation + penalize danger | no | no | ChauffeurNet |
| Noise injection DART | inject noise at collection to widen distribution | no | no | DART |
| Closed-loop training + RL fine-tune | train directly on $d_\pi$ | no | yes | Roach |
| Long-tail mining / shadow mode | targeted fill of high-risk tail | no | no | industrial practice |
| World model long-tail synthesis | generate rare scenarios | no | yes (generative) | GAIA-1 |

## 5. A Case Walkthrough: a History of "Fighting Covariate Shift"

- **ALVINN (1989)**: naive pixel→steering BC, **with no recovery mechanism at all**, fails on any deviation — the original victim of covariate shift.
- **PilotNet (2016)**: also E2E+BC, but the first to **systematically inject recovery data via three cameras / view synthesis** (§4.2), letting the car recover from deviation.
- **ChauffeurNet (2018)**: moved to a mid-to-mid modular representation, **"rehearsing disaster" in training via synthetic perturbation + auxiliary loss** (§4.3), and explicitly penalizing dangerous states.

In one line: from "ignore it" → "manufacture data" → "manufacture disaster + penalize danger" → "closed-loop training / world-model long-tail synthesis," a history of progressively cornering the same covariate-shift problem.

---

## 6. Method Comparison

See the summary table at the end of §4. In one ordering: **most deployable** — three cameras/DART/perturbation (no online expert, no sim); **closest to the essence** — closed-loop training/RL fine-tuning (train directly on $d_\pi$, but needs sim); **strongest long-tail coverage** — world-model generation (but high training/eval cost).

---

## 7. Common Pitfalls

1. **Going on-road judging only by open-loop L2**: covariate shift makes offline metrics systematically optimistic (§3.3); always evaluate closed-loop before deployment.
2. **Assuming more BC data alone cures it**: expanding data on $d_{\pi^*}$ treats the symptom, not the cause, because the problem is in states the policy never saw on $d_\pi$; DAgger/recovery is the cure.
3. **Treating recovery data as ordinary augmentation**: random augmentation that does not cover real failure modes (line-crossing, oncoming swerve, hard braking) helps little with covariate shift.
4. **Running DAgger directly on a real car**: letting an immature policy drive to collect data online has safety risk; needs SafeDAgger/human-in-the-loop backstop.
5. **Using non-reactive background traffic in closed-loop eval**: background cars that do not react to ego inflate the score; real interaction (reactive, like nuPlan's reactive track) is what exposes problems.

---

## 8. Frontier Trends

1. **Closed-loop differentiable simulation + RL fine-tuning** becomes standard for end-to-end training, removing the distribution gap at the source.
2. **Generative world models for the long tail** (GAIA series): synthesize rare dangerous scenarios for closed-loop training and evaluation.
3. **Large models / VLA bring language-level interpretable correction**: use language reasoning to recognize and explain "I am drifting."
4. **Hybrid real-sim closed-loop evaluation**: after nuPlan / CARLA, combine real-log replay with simulated reactive traffic to approach the true distribution.
