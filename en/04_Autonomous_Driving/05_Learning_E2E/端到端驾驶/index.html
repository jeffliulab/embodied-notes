    End-to-End Driving

    End-to-End Driving — the method lineage that compresses "sensors → control" into a single differentiable network, from ALVINN's pixels-to-steering, to UniAD stitching perception/prediction/planning into a differentiable whole, to GAIA-1 generating the future with a world model.

    Difficulty: Intermediate-Advanced Prerequisites: Autonomous Driving Paradigms & Method Taxonomy (the 2-axis map of architecture vs. learning), Covariate Shift & the Closed-Loop Gap (BC's \(O(\epsilon T^2)\) and recovery), Imitation Learning Further reading: VLA Models, Representations & World Models, Datasets & Closed-Loop Evaluation

    1. What End-to-End Driving Is

    End-to-end (E2E) driving means: use a single differentiable network that turns raw sensor inputs (camera/LiDAR/radar) directly into control commands or a planned trajectory, with gradients flowing all the way from the final objective back to the front-end perception features. On the 2-axis map of the paradigm taxonomy, it occupies the "architecture = end-to-end" column — but it is orthogonal to "how you train it": end-to-end can be paired with imitation learning (the vast majority), reinforcement learning (Roach), or world models (MILE/GAIA).

    First we must dismantle two false equalities — both covered in the paradigm note; here we only restate the conclusions, without re-deriving:

    • End-to-end ≠ behavior cloning (BC). End-to-end is an architectural property ("gradients flow through, one network end to end"); BC is a training property ("supervised regression on expert data"). A BC-trained modular stack is not end-to-end; an RL-trained single network is end-to-end. They are different axes.
    • End-to-end ≠ no modules. The litmus test for end-to-end is "can gradients flow from the control end back to the perception end," not "does the network contain structure." UniAD keeps the module semantics of perception/prediction/planning yet is still end-to-end (§5).

    The two core selling points of end-to-end over a modular pipeline: joint optimization (the whole system aligns to one objective, avoiding "per-module optimum ≠ global optimum") and no hand-crafted information bottleneck (perception does not prematurely discard information useful for the decision). The cost is being a hard-to-verify black box and data-hungry, and it exposes imitation learning's covariate shift most starkly — the undercurrent running through every method in this note.

    This note is the systems expansion of the paradigm note's "end-to-end quadrant" and the covariate-shift note: along one historical timeline, we look at the concrete engineering choices each generation of E2E system makes on "input modality, intermediate representation, degree of differentiability, and how it fights covariate shift."

    Figure 1 — Timeline of end-to-end driving methods. Six milestones (ALVINN→PilotNet→CILRS→TransFuser→UniAD/VAD→world models) evolve along three bands that intensify together: input modality from mono pixels to surround multi-sensor, intermediate representation from implicit to BEV/vector/occupancy/generative latent, and training paradigm from naive BC to differentiable-modular/RL/world models. Covariate shift (see Covariate Shift & the Closed-Loop Gap) is the undercurrent throughout.

    2. The Origins: Pixels to Control

    2.1 ALVINN (1989)

    ALVINN (Autonomous Land Vehicle In a Neural Network, Pomerleau 1989) is the progenitor of end-to-end driving: a 3-layer fully connected network taking a 30×32 camera grayscale image (plus a range sensor) as input and outputting a steering direction. It is the most naive "pixels → control" behavior cloning, with no recovery mechanism whatsoever — once the car deviates from the road poses seen in training, the input falls OOD, the output becomes near-random, and it fails immediately. ALVINN is the original victim of covariate shift, and the starting point for understanding every subsequent engineering patch.

    2.2 PilotNet (2016)

    Over twenty years later, PilotNet (Bojarski et al. 2016, NVIDIA) redid the same thing with a modern CNN: raw camera pixels pass through ~9 convolutional layers and directly regress the steering angle, trained end-to-end with BC. There is no qualitative architectural change; the key innovation is systematically fighting covariate shift:

    • Three cameras inject recovery: the car carries left/center/right cameras. The center camera is paired with the expert's steering as a normal sample; the off-center left/right cameras are paired with "should steer right/left to correct" labels — fabricating a large number of "deviate-then-recover" samples.
    • Viewpoint synthesis interpolation: geometric transforms synthesize additional shifted viewpoints from existing images, further expanding coverage of deviation magnitudes.

    These two tricks approximate DAgger's effect with offline data (providing supervision on the deviated states the policy will visit), and are the most naive yet effective recovery technique in engineering. Their mechanism and the derivation of "why three cameras approximate \(d_\pi\)" are detailed in Covariate Shift & the Closed-Loop Gap §4.2 and not repeated here.

    Scope of this note: the cause of covariate shift in ALVINN/PilotNet and the math of recovery are covered in detail in the covariate-shift note; here we focus on their position in the method lineage — pure pixel input, implicit intermediate representation, single-branch policy, the "zero point" of all later structured extensions.

    3. Conditional Imitation Learning

    Naive PilotNet has a fundamental ambiguity: at a fork, the expert's "turn left" and "turn right" are both valid demonstrations, but BC averages them into going straight — mode collapse of multi-modal demonstrations. Conditional Imitation Learning (CIL; Codevilla et al. 2018) resolves this by adding a high-level navigation command \(c\) (straight/left/right/follow-lane) as an extra conditioning input, so the network learns a conditional policy \(\pi(a \mid o, c)\), disambiguating the fork.

    The engineering realization is a branched structure: a shared perception backbone extracts observation features, which are routed by command \(c\) to the corresponding command-specific branch head (one sub-network per command); only the selected branch contributes to that sample's loss. This way straight/left/right each learn a clean unimodal policy, no longer averaging one another:

    \[ a = \text{Branch}_{c}\big(f_{\text{enc}}(o)\big), \qquad c \in \{\text{straight, left, right, follow}\} \]

    CILRS (Codevilla et al. 2019, "Exploring the Limitations of Behavior Cloning") added two things on top: ① a ResNet perception backbone for better representations; ② a speed-prediction auxiliary head (predicting current vehicle speed) to mitigate the "inertia problem" — BC models tend to learn the spurious correlation "moving last frame → keep moving this frame" and fail to stop when they should. CILRS was also among the first to systematically reveal the ceiling of BC (data quantity, initialization variance, inertia bias), complementing the empirical findings in the covariate-shift note.

    The significance of CIL/CILRS: end-to-end gained an explicit high-level interface (navigation commands) for the first time, letting "sensors → control, one network" still obey routing commands. This "condition on language/commands" line later leads straight to VLA models feeding natural-language instructions directly into the driving policy.

    4. Multi-Sensor Fusion End-to-End

    Monocular-camera end-to-end struggles at night, in strong backlight, and at long-range ranging. TransFuser (Prakash et al. 2021 CVPR; extended TPAMI 2022) pushes end-to-end to multi-sensor fusion: camera (semantically rich but scale-free) + LiDAR BEV (precise geometry but sparse) exchange information bidirectionally at multiple scales via Transformer attention.

    The key design is geometry-agnostic attention fusion: conventional fusion (early/late concatenation, geometric projection) relies on precise sensor calibration and projection geometry, whereas TransFuser inserts Transformer modules at several intermediate layers of the convolutional backbone, letting the camera feature map and the LiDAR BEV feature map query each other via self-attention — the model itself learns "which patch of LiDAR geometry this camera pixel corresponds to," with no hard-coded projection. This matters especially for global context (e.g., a crossing vehicle at an occluded intersection, or correlating a distant red light with nearby traffic), because attention naturally models long-range dependencies.

    On the output side, TransFuser uses the fused features for GRU autoregressive waypoint prediction (outputting future waypoints rather than a direct steering angle), then tracked by classical lateral/longitudinal controllers (PID / pure pursuit). It substantially outperforms pure-camera baselines on the CARLA closed-loop leaderboard, particularly lowering collision rates at complex intersections — confirming "the smaller the information bottleneck and the fuller the global context, the steadier the closed loop."

    Note: TransFuser's input side (camera/LiDAR BEV, occupancy, etc. representations) shares a representation language with the perception modules in the paradigm taxonomy (BEVFormer/LSS/PointPillars, etc.); this note does not expand perception details, only how it is jointly optimized within end-to-end.

    5. End-to-End Differentiable, Modular

    5.1 UniAD: A Differentiable Whole with Planning as the Objective

    UniAD (Hu et al. 2023, CVPR Best Paper) is a milestone of the end-to-end paradigm, and the most forceful proof that "end-to-end ≠ no modules." UniAD keeps the full module semantics — multi-object tracking, map segmentation, motion prediction, occupancy prediction, planning — but stitches them into a single differentiable whole using queries as the unified interface, with gradients backpropagated under the final planning objective.

    Three core ideas:

    1. Planning-oriented: a conventional modular stack lets each module optimize its own metric (perception chases mAP, prediction chases minADE), but improving these metrics does not necessarily translate to better planning. UniAD inverts this, making all upstream modules serve the downstream planner, with the planning loss's gradient flowing back to every module — perception/prediction learn representations that are "useful for planning," not isolated metrics.
    2. Query-based connection, intermediate supervision retained: modules are not joined by hand-crafted boxes/trajectories as hard interfaces, but pass information via transformer queries (track query, map query, motion query, occupancy query), avoiding the information bottleneck of hard interfaces; meanwhile each module still keeps its own intermediate supervision (ground-truth for tracking/segmentation/prediction), stabilizing training and preserving interpretability.
    3. Gradients flow through: this is exactly the litmus test for end-to-end — gradients flow from the planning end all the way to the perception end. So UniAD is both modular (clear module boundaries and intermediate supervision) and end-to-end (gradients flow through, joint optimization), landing in the "middle ground" of paradigm taxonomy §2.3.

    Figure 2 — UniAD end-to-end differentiable modular structure. After multi-sensor input is BEV-encoded, TrackFormer/MapFormer/MotionFormer/OccFormer are forward-chained with queries as the interface (thin arrows), each module still keeping its own intermediate supervision (gold dots); the final planning-loss gradient backpropagates along the whole chain to perception (orange dashed) — i.e., planning-oriented end-to-end. This figure is the systems-level expansion of "end-to-end differentiable modular" in the paradigm taxonomy §2.3.

    5.2 VAD: Vectorized Scene Representation

    VAD (Jiang et al. 2023 ICCV) follows the same "differentiable modular" road, but swaps the scene representation from a dense grid to vectors (vectorized): it represents map elements (lane lines, boundaries) and agent trajectories both as vector entities (polylines/point sets), rather than occupancy grids or dense BEV feature maps.

    Two benefits of vectorization: ① lighter and faster — no need to process per-pixel convolutions of a dense grid, significantly accelerating inference and getting closer to real-time deployment; ② natively encodes structured constraints — the ego's relations to lanes/boundaries/other agents can be expressed directly with geometric constraints between vectors (collision, out-of-boundary, alignment with lane direction), serving as differentiable regularizers for planning. VAD matches or even surpasses UniAD's planning metrics on nuScenes end-to-end planning at lower latency, the standard-bearer of the vector camp in the "dense vs. vector" representation debate.

    6. The World-Model Route

    The systems above are all discriminative (observation → action). The world-model route introduces generative thinking: have the network learn an environment dynamics model that predicts the future \(p(o_{t+1} \mid o_{\le t}, a_t)\), and plan or train policies within an imagined future. Its motivation is of one piece with Representations & World Models in robotics.

    6.1 MILE: Latent Imagination in a BEV Latent Space

    MILE (Hu et al. 2022 NeurIPS) brings the world model into driving: it learns a latent dynamics model in a BEV latent space from cameras, jointly learning perception representations and the transition of future latent states purely from offline expert data (no online simulation interaction needed), then imagines and learns a policy within this latent space. It proves the world-model route can be trained fully offline and achieve strong CARLA closed-loop performance — a standard-bearer of "model-based + imitation."

    6.2 GAIA-1: A Generative Driving World Model

    GAIA-1 (Hu et al. 2023, arXiv:2309.17080, Wayve) is a large-scale generative driving world model: it encodes video, action, and text into tokens, and uses an autoregressive transformer to predict the next image token, thereby being able to generate realistic multi-minute driving videos conditioned on action/language. Its value lies not in directly driving, but in two things —

    1. Generate long-tail scenarios: synthesizing rare hazardous scenarios hard to collect in the real world (jaywalking, hard braking, rare weather) as recovery training data or closed-loop evaluation environments, bypassing the risk and cost of real collection (see Covariate Shift & the Closed-Loop Gap §4.7).
    2. Learn a generalizable world prior: generative pretraining forces the model to understand physics and causality (cars move, people yield, red means stop), and this prior transfers to downstream policies.

    This world-model line is rapidly merging with generative VLA models and video generation, and is a candidate for the "next paradigm" of end-to-end.

    7. End-to-End RL

    Another orthogonal branch of end-to-end is training with RL instead of BC — which fights covariate shift at the root: RL has the policy visit its own induced \(d_\pi\) during closed-loop rollouts, with rewards directly penalizing out-of-distribution dangerous behavior, making "training distribution = deployment distribution" a reality. The difficulty is that real-world RL is too dangerous/slow, so it is usually done in simulation.

    Roach ("Reinforcement Learning Coach," Zhang et al. 2021 ICCV) is the classic paradigm:

    1. Train an RL coach: it enjoys privileged information (BEV ground-truth map, exact states of other vehicles — i.e., a god's-eye view), and learns a very strong driving policy with RL (PPO, etc.) in CARLA closed loop. Because of the privileged input, it learns fast and well.
    2. Distill to a camera student: have a camera-only end-to-end student imitate this RL coach's action distribution and latent features. The coach can supply the "correct action" at any state the student visits, acting as an always-online, zero-cost, zero-risk expert — precisely filling the gap of DAgger's "online expert queries are expensive and dangerous" on a real car (cf. Covariate Shift & the Closed-Loop Gap §4.1/§4.5).

    This "privileged-coach RL + distill to deployment sensors" paradigm (the same idea originates in Learning by Cheating, Chen et al. 2019) is a common recipe for top end-to-end systems on the current CARLA leaderboard, landing in the "end-to-end RL" quadrant of the paradigm taxonomy.

    Method Comparison

    System Year Input modality Intermediate rep. Differentiability/Architecture Training paradigm Fighting covariate shift
    ALVINN 1989 mono grayscale implicit single net BC none
    PilotNet 2016 mono camera implicit single net BC 3 cameras / viewpoint synth.
    CIL / CILRS 2018/19 mono camera implicit branched single net conditional BC speed head / inertia mitigation
    TransFuser 2021 camera+LiDAR multi-scale fused features single net (Tx fusion) BC (waypoint) multi-sensor reduces uncertainty
    UniAD 2023 surround cam (±LiDAR) BEV+query differentiable modular imitation (planning-oriented) global rep. + closed-loop eval
    VAD 2023 surround camera vector differentiable modular imitation + geometric constraints vector-constraint regularizers
    MILE 2022 camera BEV latent dynamics generative imitation + latent imagination model-based offline training
    GAIA-1 2023 video+action+text generative latent generative autoregressive generation long-tail synth. / world prior
    Roach 2021 camera (student) implicit single net RL coach → distill closed-loop RL + always-online expert

    One-line ordering: earliest and most naive ALVINN/PilotNet (pixels → control) → gaining a high-level interface CIL/CILRS (command conditioning) → fuller information TransFuser (multi-sensor fusion) → both modular and end-to-end UniAD/VAD (differentiable modular, planning-oriented) → toward generative/closed-loop world models + end-to-end RL (aligning distributions at the root).

    Common Pitfalls

    1. Treating "end-to-end" as "no modules": the litmus test for end-to-end is gradient flow-through, not whether the network contains structure. UniAD keeps full module semantics yet is still end-to-end (§1, §5); see paradigm taxonomy §2.3.
    2. Equating "end-to-end" with "behavior cloning": architecture (end-to-end/modular) and training (BC/RL/world model) are two orthogonal axes. Roach is RL-trained end-to-end; MILE is world-model-trained end-to-end.
    3. Selecting end-to-end models by open-loop L2 only: open-loop replay always locks the model on the expert distribution and cannot measure covariate shift; closed-loop evaluation (CARLA/nuPlan) is mandatory before deployment. See the derivation in Covariate Shift & the Closed-Loop Gap §3.
    4. Ignoring multi-modal ambiguity / the inertia problem: at a fork without navigation-command conditioning, BC averages into going straight (§3); without handling the speed spurious correlation, you get an "won't stop when it should" inertia bias (CILRS).
    5. Thinking more data alone makes pure-BC end-to-end indefinitely better: CILRS has empirically shown BC has a ceiling (diminishing data returns, high variance, inertia bias); the cure is recovery data / closed-loop training / RL, not just piling on data.
    6. Confusing the "privileged coach" with the "deployment model": the Roach/LbC coach uses privileged input only to produce supervision and cannot be deployed directly (the deployment model has only on-board sensors); forgetting the distillation step means the problem is not solved.

    Frontier Trends

    1. Differentiable modular becomes the mainstream skeleton: after UniAD/VAD, the planning-oriented end-to-end differentiable stack (keep module semantics + gradient flow-through + intermediate supervision) becomes the common denominator in research and production.
    2. Occupancy + vector hybrid representations: dense occupancy (fine-grained geometry, handling arbitrary-shape obstacles) and vectors (lightweight, structured constraints) converge to balance accuracy and latency.
    3. Generative world models merge with VLA: GAIA-family world models converge with generative VLA and video generation, both synthesizing long-tail data and serving as closed-loop evaluation environments, while bringing language-level interpretable reasoning.
    4. End-to-end RL / closed-loop fine-tuning standardizes: privileged-coach RL + distillation, and differentiable closed-loop fine-tuning, eliminate covariate shift at the root and gradually replace pure offline BC.
    5. Interpretability and verifiability: the pain of end-to-end being a hard-to-verify black box is being mitigated with intermediate supervision, language reasoning (VLA explaining "why I drive this way"), and formal safety shells.
