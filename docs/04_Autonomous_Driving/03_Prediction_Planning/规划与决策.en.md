# Planning & Decision-Making — From Behavior to Motion Trajectories

> *Planning & Decision-Making — autonomous driving splits "how should I drive" into two layers: **first a discrete behavior decision (change lane? yield? overtake?)**, then a **continuous motion trajectory** satisfying dynamics, comfort and safety constraints, finally handed to a controller to track. This note clarifies the boundary between the two layers, the method spectrum (FSM/POMDP, sampling/optimization/MPC), cost-function design, and RSS-style verifiable safety.*
>
> **Difficulty**: Intermediate-Advanced
> **Prerequisites**: [Motion Prediction](运动预测.en.md), [Motion Planning](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/运动规划.en.md), [MPC](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/MPC.en.md)

## 1. Two Planning Layers (behavior + motion)

The decision & planning module sits **after prediction, before control**. It ingests: ego state, map and drivable area, and the future trajectory distributions of other agents from perception/[prediction](运动预测.en.md); it outputs a time-stamped **reference trajectory** $\{(x_k, y_k, \theta_k, v_k, t_k)\}$ for [vehicle control](../04_Control/车辆控制.en.md) to track.

Almost every production stack splits this into two layers, because their mathematical nature is fundamentally different:

| Layer | Decision object | Mathematical nature | Time scale | Typical methods |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Behavior** | Discrete semantic action: keep-lane / change-lane / yield / cross intersection | Discrete, combinatorial, game-theoretic | 1–8 s | FSM, rules, POMDP, search |
| **Motion** | Continuous trajectory $\xi(t)$ | Continuous, constrained optimization | 0.1–5 s | sampling, lattice, optimization, MPC |

**Why split?** Searching "whether to change lanes" directly in continuous space makes the optimization strongly non-convex and multi-modal (each discrete choice is its own attraction basin). The behavior layer first **commits to a semantic intent** (e.g. "merge left and slot in behind car A"), collapsing the multi-modal problem into a **single-modal, near-convex** trajectory optimization that the motion layer can solve stably and fast. The cost: a layered split loses information (a wrong upper-layer choice cannot be rescued below), motivating two reconciliation routes — **joint behavior+motion** (e.g. lattice enumerating both intent and trajectory) and **end-to-end**.

## 2. Behavior Decision (FSM, rules, POMDP under uncertainty)

### 2.1 Finite State Machine (FSM)

The most classic and easiest-to-ship behavior decider. Driving semantic states are enumerated as nodes (`LANE_KEEP`, `LANE_CHANGE_LEFT`, `STOP_FOR_LIGHT`, `YIELD`, `OVERTAKE`…), with guard conditions defining transitions. The DARPA Urban Challenge-era Junior/Boss and early Apollo's `scenario` dispatch are essentially hierarchical FSMs.

- **Pros**: readable, auditable, predictable behavior, easy to test.
- **Cons**: states/transitions **explode exponentially** with scenario combinations ("unprotected left turn + pedestrian + cyclist + rain"), guards conflict; hard to express continuous uncertainty.

In practice it degrades to **hierarchical FSM + behavior tree** to tame combinatorial complexity.

### 2.2 Rule / cost-table decision

Instead of an explicit state machine, **enumerate candidate semantic actions** (e.g. for each lane generate "keep/left/right"), compute a scalar cost per action (throughput efficiency, conflict with others, lane-change benefit), and take the argmin. This is the embryo of a lattice planner at the behavior layer, and the transitional form softening behavior decision into optimization.

### 2.3 POMDP — decision under uncertainty

In real driving the intent of other agents is **unobservable**: is the neighbor merging or just drifting? Will the pedestrian suddenly cross? This is naturally a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP).

$$
\langle \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{T}, \mathcal{R}, \Omega, \mathcal{O}, \gamma \rangle, \qquad
b'(s') \propto \mathcal{O}(o\mid s',a)\sum_{s} \mathcal{T}(s'\mid s,a)\, b(s)
$$

The agent decides not on state $s$ but on a **belief** $b$ (state distribution), maximizing expected discounted return $\mathbb{E}[\sum_t \gamma^t \mathcal{R}]$. Its power lies in automatically trading off **information gathering vs task progress** — e.g. at an occluded intersection, creep forward to "peek" and shrink belief entropy before committing, exactly what a human driver does.

Exact POMDP solving is PSPACE-hard; practice uses approximations:

- **QMDP / FIB**: ignore future observations, reducing belief decisions to weighted MDP solutions.
- **DESPOT / POMCP**: Monte-Carlo belief tree search, online expansion of a limited set of determinized samples; the mainstream approximator for unprotected left turns, merges, and other interactive scenarios.
- **Multi-policy (MPDM / EUDM)**: enumerate a few "macro-policies" (ego policy × hypothesized agent intent), forward-simulate and score; a compromise between lattice/FSM and POMDP, engineering-friendly.

> Relation to [motion prediction](运动预测.en.md): prediction supplies $\mathcal{T}$ and $\mathcal{O}$ (others' future distribution), and decision plans on top. Treating prediction as "frozen ground truth" causes the **freezing robot problem** — prediction fills all space and planning finds no path; the cure is **interactive/conditional prediction** (prediction conditioned on the ego plan) or game-theoretic joint planning.

## 3. Motion Planning Methods (sampling, lattice, optimization, MPC)

After committing to an intent, motion planning solves a trajectory satisfying vehicle dynamics $\dot{x}=f(x,u)$, collision-free, with minimal cost. Four method families:

### 3.1 Sampling: RRT* and kinodynamic variants

Randomly sample, extend, and backtrack in state space (see [Motion Planning](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/运动规划.en.md)). RRT* is asymptotically optimal; used for parking, U-turns, unstructured scenes (no lane lines). The difficulty is **satisfying nonholonomic constraints** (a car cannot slide sideways) — requiring kinodynamic RRT or Reeds-Shepp/Dubins curves for STEER.

### 3.2 Lattice / Hybrid A*

The workhorse for structured roads (highways, urban arterials).

- **Lattice planner**: in the **Frenet frame** (arc length $s$ along lane, lateral offset $d$), regularly **sample** terminal states $(s,d,\dot s)$ and connect with **quintic polynomials** to generate many candidate trajectories, then evaluate each for collision/constraints/cost and pick the best. Apollo's EM planner and Werling's lateral-longitudinal decoupling are representative. The Frenet frame's advantage: "hug the lane" is encoded into the coordinate system, decoupling lateral and longitudinal optimization.
- **Hybrid A\***: discrete search over continuous poses respecting the minimum turning radius; the standard choice for parking and narrow passages.

### 3.3 Optimization: treat the trajectory itself as the variable

Discretize the trajectory into waypoints $\xi=\{x_k\}$, minimize a cost functional under constraints:

$$
\min_{\xi}\ \sum_k \big[ w_s\, c_{\text{safe}}(x_k) + w_c\, \|\ddot{\xi}_k\|^2 + w_e\, c_{\text{eff}}(x_k)\big]
\quad \text{s.t.}\ x_{k+1}=f(x_k,u_k),\ g(x_k)\le 0
$$

Non-convex collision constraints are linearized via sequential convex optimization (TrajOpt idea); driving commonly uses **iLQR / DDP** (second-order expansion along the trajectory, fast with warm-start) or QP (millisecond-level after convexification). Pros: smooth trajectories, explicit constraints; cons: dependence on a good initial guess (provided by sampling/lattice), prone to local optima.

### 3.4 Model Predictive Control (MPC)

Make "receding-horizon optimization + execute only the first step" the planning core: each cycle predict $H$ steps ahead, solve the optimal control sequence, execute $u_0$, then re-solve next cycle with new observations. Driving typically uses a horizon of 3–5 s, frequency 10–50 Hz, with cost over distance to the reference line, squared acceleration/jerk, squared gap to others, and constraints on velocity/acceleration limits and lane boundaries. Tube MPC / robust MPC handle prediction and model uncertainty.

→ Full derivation, iLQR, linear/nonlinear/Tube taxonomy and stability are in the **[MPC note](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/MPC.en.md)**. MPC can serve as a motion planner (outputting trajectories) or as a low-level tracker (see [Vehicle Control](../04_Control/车辆控制.en.md)), with different horizons and frequencies.

## 4. Cost-Function Design (comfort / safety / efficiency / compliance trade-off)

Planning quality is 90% determined by the cost function. A typical weighted sum:

$$
J(\xi) = w_s\, J_{\text{safety}} + w_c\, J_{\text{comfort}} + w_e\, J_{\text{efficiency}} + w_r\, J_{\text{rules}}
$$

| Objective | Common terms | Engineering meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **safety** | min distance to others/boundary, collision TTC, occupancy-probability integral | usually a hard constraint or very high-weight soft term |
| **comfort** | $\int \|\ddot{\xi}\|^2$ (accel), $\int \dddot{\xi}^2$ (jerk), lateral accel | directly affects ride feel and motion sickness |
| **efficiency** | travel time, deviation from desired speed, lane-change benefit | inherently conflicts with safety/comfort |
| **rules** | speed limit, lane lines, yield priority, stop line | partly hard-codable in some jurisdictions |

**Core difficulties and remedies:**

- **Incommensurable objectives**: weighting "meters", "seconds", "m/s³" into one sum lacks physical meaning. Remedies: lexicographic ordering (satisfy safety first, then optimize comfort), normalization, or constraints instead of soft terms.
- **Weight-tuning explosion**: hand-tuned $w$ fail to balance across long-tail scenarios. Remedy: **inverse RL / inverse optimal control (IRL/IOC)** to recover cost from human driving (ChauffeurNet, MaxEnt IRL); or learned cost (learned planners on nuPlan).
- **Cost ≠ true intent**: a low-cost trajectory may be "legal but inhuman" (hard-braking for a plastic bag). Needs scenario-specific cost and risk grading.

## 5. Safety & Interpretability (verifiable bounds, Responsibility-Sensitive Safety RSS)

Learned planners are black boxes; production needs **verifiable safety bounds**, not just average metrics.

### 5.1 Responsibility-Sensitive Safety (RSS)

A formal framework from Mobileye (Shalev-Shwartz et al. 2017): a few interpretable rules give closed-form bounds such as the **minimum safe distance**; as long as the ego does not violate them, the ego is not at fault in a collision. The most representative is the longitudinal safe distance:

$$
d_{\min} = \Big[ v_r \rho + \tfrac{1}{2} a_{\max}\rho^2 + \tfrac{(v_r + \rho a_{\max})^2}{2 b_{\min}} - \tfrac{v_f^2}{2 b_{\max}} \Big]_+
$$

where $v_r,v_f$ are rear/front vehicle speeds, $\rho$ the response time, $a_{\max}$ max acceleration, $b_{\min},b_{\max}$ minimum/maximum reasonable deceleration. Intuition: keep enough distance that, even if the ego accelerates over the worst-case response time and the lead brakes hardest, the ego can still stop. RSS gives rules for lateral, longitudinal, intersection, and occlusion scenarios, usable as a **safety shield** on the planner — any output trajectory must pass the RSS check, otherwise fall back to a conservative action.

### 5.2 Other verifiable mechanisms

- **Reachability analysis / control barrier function (CBF)**: forward reachable sets or CBFs give formal guarantees on state constraints, encoding safety as hard constraints in the optimization.
- **Safety shield / fallback**: a learned planner's output is filtered by a rule/MPC shield; on violation it switches to a minimal risk maneuver (MRM).
- **Interpretable cost**: decompose cost into named terms for easy post-incident attribution.

> Trade-off: worst-case bounds like RSS are **conservative** (may over-yield, get cut off), conflicting with efficiency; the research direction is to data-drivenly tighten worst-case assumptions (e.g. distributional estimation of others' braking ability).

## 6. Adversarial & Long-Tail Scenarios

Real planning difficulty concentrates in the distribution tail:

- **Interaction and games**: unprotected left turns, merges, roundabouts — others' behavior depends on the ego's action; one-way prediction+planning **freezes** or is **over-conservative**. Remedies: game-theoretic planning (Stackelberg/Nash), conditional prediction, MPDM multi-policy forward simulation.
- **Occlusion and limited field of view**: a pedestrian crossing behind a large vehicle. Remedies: worst-case assumptions over **invisible regions** (phantom agents), or POMDP active information gathering (creep to peek).
- **Long-tail rare events**: wrong-way vehicles, fallen cargo, construction reroutes, special vehicles. Supervised samples are scarce; needs simulation synthesis (CARLA-like) + adversarial scenario generation.
- **Adversarial perturbations**: adversarial patches at the perception layer pollute prediction and mislead planning; planning must be robust to upstream uncertainty (robust/risk-aware planning, sensitive to prediction covariance).
- **Closed-loop vs open-loop evaluation**: open-loop only compares single-frame similarity to human trajectories (ADE/FDE), **rewarding imitation and masking compounding errors**; closed-loop simulation evaluation (nuPlan, CARLA leaderboard) is mandatory to expose drift and causal confusion.

## Method Comparison

| Dimension | FSM / rules | POMDP / search | Lattice | Optimization / MPC | End-to-end learning |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Decision layer | behavior | behavior | behavior+motion | motion | merged |
| Handles uncertainty | weak (hard guards) | strong (belief) | medium (multi-candidate) | medium (robust/tube) | implicit (data) |
| Trajectory quality | depends on downstream | depends on downstream | medium (sampling resolution) | high (smooth, explicit constraints) | unstable |
| Interpretable/auditable | high | medium | high | medium | low |
| Real-time | very fast | slow (online search) | fast | medium (convex fast, non-convex slow) | fast (after inference) |
| Long-tail robustness | poor (combinatorial blowup) | fair | medium | medium | depends on data coverage |
| Representative | Boss/Junior, Apollo scenario | DESPOT, MPDM/EUDM | Apollo EM, Werling | iLQR, Tube-MPC | UniAD, VAD |

## Common Pitfalls

- **Blind layering loses information**: if the behavior layer chooses wrong (e.g. "grab when it should yield"), no amount of motion optimization can recover it. Align behavior cost with motion cost, or add joint/fallback mechanisms.
- **Freezing robot**: treating prediction as frozen ground truth without interaction, the planner finds no feasible solution and stalls in dense scenes. Use conditional/interactive prediction or game-theoretic planning.
- **Open-loop metric self-deception**: tuning only on ADE/FDE or imitation loss drifts and explodes with compounding errors on real roads. Closed-loop evaluation (nuPlan/CARLA) is mandatory.
- **Cost weights overfit the demo**: hand-tuned $w$ look great on recorded clips but collapse on the long tail. Use IRL/learned cost + large-scale closed-loop regression.
- **Safety soft constraint overpowered by efficiency**: making collision a soft term that a high efficiency weight can offset. Safety should be a hard constraint or an inviolable bound with an RSS shield.
- **Frenet frame distorts at high curvature**: with sharp curves or missing lane lines the Frenet projection is ill-conditioned and the lateral-longitudinal decoupling assumption breaks. Fall back to Cartesian or Hybrid A*.
- **MPC horizon/frequency mismatch**: too-short horizon is myopic, too-long accumulates model error; do not confuse the horizon/frequency of the planning MPC and the tracking MPC.

## Frontier Trends

- **End-to-end planning**: UniAD (Hu 2023, CVPR Best Paper) unifies detection-tracking-mapping-prediction-planning into one differentiable pipeline, supervising the full stack with planning as the goal; VAD (Jiang 2023, ICCV) uses a vectorized scene representation for efficient end-to-end planning. Closes the layered information loss but sacrifices interpretability.
- **Learned cost / closed-loop imitation**: nuPlan (Caesar 2021) pushes from "open-loop ADE" to "closed-loop planning evaluation"; learned planner + rule shield becomes the compromise paradigm.
- **World-model-driven planning**: MILE (Hu 2022, NeurIPS) and GAIA-1 (Hu 2023) learn generative driving world models, letting planners roll out in imagination (MPC idea × generative model) and improving long-tail coverage.
- **Game-theoretic and interactive planning**: explicitly modeling planning as a multi-agent game alleviates freezing/over-conservatism, gradually reaching production merges and unprotected left turns.
- **Data-driven formal safety**: combining RSS, CBF, reachability with learning to data-drivenly tighten worst-case assumptions, finding a better frontier between safety and efficiency.
