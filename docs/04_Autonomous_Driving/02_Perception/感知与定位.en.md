# Perception & Localization

> *Perception & Localization — the autonomous car's "eyes" and "GPS on steroids": turn raw multi-sensor signals into a single, planning-consumable BEV/occupancy world model, and know the ego vehicle's centimeter-level pose within the map.*
>
> **Difficulty**: Intermediate
> **Prerequisites**: [Autonomous Driving Paradigms & Method Taxonomy](../01_Foundations/自动驾驶范式与方法谱系.en.md), [SLAM: Simultaneous Localization and Mapping](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/SLAM.en.md), [Coordinate Frames](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/坐标系.en.md)
> **Further reading**: [End-to-End Driving](../05_Learning_E2E/端到端驾驶.en.md), [Datasets & Closed-Loop Evaluation](../06_Data_Eval/数据集与闭环评测.en.md)

---

Perception answers "**what is around me, where is it, where is it going**"; localization answers "**where am I in the world/map and what is my heading**". In the modular pipeline of the [paradigm taxonomy](../01_Foundations/自动驾驶范式与方法谱系.en.md) these are the two front-most blocks; even in end-to-end differentiable systems like UniAD they survive as **explicit BEV/occupancy intermediate representations**. This note follows one spine — "from single sensor to a unified BEV world model" — chaining camera 3D detection, LiDAR detection, occupancy grids, multi-sensor fusion, online mapping, and integrated navigation / SLAM registration into a single logical thread.

---

## 1. Perception Tasks Overview (2D/3D Detection, Semantic/Panoptic Segmentation, Tracking)

Driving perception is not one task but a set of complementary ones; classifying by **output representation** is clearest:

| Task | Output | Typical use | Metric |
|---|---|---|---|
| **2D detection** | image-plane bbox + class | traffic lights, sign recognition | mAP@IoU |
| **3D detection** | 9-DoF box in ego frame $(x,y,z,w,l,h,\theta,v_x,v_y)$ | obstacles, feeding boxes to prediction/planning | nuScenes mAP / NDS |
| **Semantic segmentation** | per-pixel/per-voxel class | drivable area, lanes, terrain | mIoU |
| **Panoptic segmentation** | semantics + instances (stuff + things) | both "what" and "which one" | PQ |
| **Multi-object tracking (MOT)** | cross-frame consistent track IDs + trajectories | history + velocity for prediction | AMOTA / MOTA / IDF1 |
| **Occupancy prediction** | voxel-level (occupied + semantics [+ flow]) | long-tail geometry, unknown obstacles | mIoU / RayIoU |

Key idea: modern production stacks **unify their outputs onto the BEV (bird's-eye-view) plane or 3D voxels** rather than staying in the perspective image. Three reasons — planning is most natural on the BEV; multi-camera/multi-sensor signals can only fuse once projected into one frame; the time dimension (velocity, history) is a translation rather than a perspective warp in BEV. The $v_x,v_y$ in 3D detection reflect that benchmarks like nuScenes require **jointly estimating velocity**, since downstream prediction/planning consume it directly.

The classic tracking paradigm is **tracking-by-detection**: detect per frame, then link detections into tracks with a motion model (Kalman filter) + data association (Hungarian / greedy). CenterPoint reuses the velocity vector from its center heatmap for cheap frame-to-frame greedy association, getting MOT almost for free.

## 2. Camera 3D Detection and the BEV Representation

Cameras are cheap and semantically rich, but monocular cameras lack depth — the core difficulty of 3D detection is **recovering 3D metric information from a 2D perspective image**.

### 2.1 The Perspective → BEV Geometry

The pinhole camera model projects a 3D point $X_c=(X,Y,Z)$ to a pixel:

$$
\lambda \begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = K\,[R\mid t]\, \begin{bmatrix} X \\ Y \\ Z \\ 1 \end{bmatrix},\qquad K=\begin{bmatrix} f_x & 0 & c_x \\ 0 & f_y & c_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

Depth $\lambda=Z$ is **divided out** during projection — that is why back-projecting a single image into 3D is ill-posed: one pixel maps to an entire ray $\{X_c=\lambda K^{-1}[u,v,1]^\top\}$. Sending image features to BEV is fundamentally about **placing/aggregating features along this ray**. Two mainstream routes:

- **Forward (lift / push)**: estimate a **depth distribution** per pixel and "scatter" features along the ray onto the BEV grid — LSS.
- **Backward (pull / sample)**: pre-define queries on the BEV grid, back-project them into each image to sample features — BEVFormer.

### 2.2 LSS: Lift-Splat-Shoot

LSS (Philion & Fidler, ECCV 2020) has three steps:

1. **Lift**: for each pixel a CNN outputs a context feature $c\in\mathbb{R}^{C}$ and a discrete **depth probability distribution** $\alpha\in\Delta^{|D|}$ (over preset depth bins $D=\{d_1,\dots,d_k\}$). The feature at depth $d_j$ is the outer product $\alpha_j\, c$, so a single pixel is "lifted" into a column of 3D frustum points. This **soft depth** (no hard single depth) is what distinguishes LSS from earlier methods.
2. **Splat**: transform all frustum points to the ego frame via camera extrinsics and **sum-pool** them onto the BEV grid (pillar accumulation). A cumulative-sum trick on sorted points sums efficiently, avoiding an explicit voxel loop.
3. **Shoot**: run downstream tasks on the resulting BEV feature map (segmentation / detection / "shoot" out trajectories for planning).

Its elegance is being **end-to-end differentiable** — the depth distribution is learned implicitly with no depth-GT supervision (though LiDAR depth supervision helps a lot, as in BEVDepth).

### 2.3 BEVFormer: Spatiotemporal Attention

BEVFormer (Li et al., ECCV 2022) takes the backward route, maintaining a set of learnable **BEV queries** $Q\in\mathbb{R}^{H\times W\times C}$, each bound to a BEV grid location:

- **Spatial cross-attention**: each BEV query samples several 3D reference points along height, projects them into each camera image via the projection matrix, and applies **deformable attention** only on the hit views. This avoids LSS's explicit depth estimation, leaving "depth ambiguity" to attention to resolve implicitly.
- **Temporal self-attention**: the previous frame's BEV feature is warped by ego-motion and attended together with the current BEV query. Temporal stacking lets the model **triangulate depth and velocity** across frames, especially helpful for occlusion and slow-moving targets.

## 3. LiDAR Detection (Point-Cloud Representations and anchor-free)

LiDAR directly gives a **metrically accurate sparse 3D point cloud** $\{(x,y,z,\text{intensity})\}$ with no depth ambiguity, but the cloud is **unordered, sparse, and drops sharply in density with range**. The core design question is "how to encode unordered points into a regular tensor that convolutions/Transformers can ingest."

### 3.1 Voxelization: VoxelNet and PointPillars

- **VoxelNet** (Zhou & Tuzel, CVPR 2018): cut space into regular 3D voxels; inside each voxel a small **VFE (voxel feature encoding)** PointNet encodes a variable number of points into a fixed-length vector, followed by 3D convolution + RPN to output boxes. The problem: 3D convolution is **expensive**, and most voxels along z are empty.
- **PointPillars** (Lang et al., CVPR 2019): the key engineering simplification — only cut **pillars in (x,y)**, with no further subdivision along z. Points in a pillar pass a PointNet to get a pillar feature, scattered back into a **2D pseudo-image**, then detected with a pure **2D CNN**. The cost is lost fine z-resolution, but the payoff is an order-of-magnitude speedup (>60 FPS); it remains a strong real-time production baseline.

Intuitive contrast: VoxelNet is "3D voxel + 3D conv", PointPillars is "2D pillar + 2D conv" — flattening the expensive third dimension.

### 3.2 CenterPoint: anchor-free

Anchor-based detectors are awkward in 3D: object orientations are arbitrary, and presetting anchors of varied heading and size is both costly and hard to match. **CenterPoint** (Yin et al., CVPR 2021) borrows from 2D CenterNet, recasting detection as **keypoint (center) regression**:

1. on the BEV feature map predict a per-class **center heatmap**; peaks are object centers;
2. at peak locations regress sub-pixel offset, $z$ height, 3D size $(w,l,h)$, heading $\theta$ (via $(\sin\theta,\cos\theta)$ to avoid angle wrap-around), and **velocity** $(v_x,v_y)$;
3. a second stage (two-stage) refines using point features at box-face centers.

Benefits of anchor-free: rotated targets no longer need rotated-anchor matching; the velocity head gives **built-in tracking** (project the current center back to the previous frame via $v$ for greedy association). CenterPoint is the de facto backbone for current LiDAR / fusion detection.

## 4. Occupancy Network

The fundamental limit of 3D boxes: they assume the world is made of **rigid cuboids of known classes**. But real roads carry **irregular and long-tail geometry** — a fallen tire, construction barrels, an overturned truck, a plastic bag, rebar protruding from a bed. Box models miss or mis-box objects of "unseen class / non-cuboid shape".

**Occupancy networks** (e.g. OccNet / TPVFormer / SurroundOcc, 2023) switch to a **class-agnostic geometric representation**: discretize the space around the ego into voxels $V\in\{0,1\}^{X\times Y\times Z}$ and predict whether each voxel is occupied (optionally with semantics, optionally with occupancy flow / velocity). It essentially carries the idea of image/2D segmentation into a 3D dense voxel grid — "can I drive through this chunk of space" without first asking "what class is this".

Why more general:

- **Long-tail geometry handled**: no reliance on class priors; any-shape obstacle is represented as long as it "occupies space", naturally handling open-set obstacles.
- **Expresses free space and the unseen**: voxels can be labeled free / occupied / unknown, so planning can explicitly exploit "known drivable space" and stay conservative about unobserved regions.
- **Natural planning interface**: collision checking is inherently a voxel/grid query, so the occupancy map feeds the cost map directly.

The cost is **dense voxels with large compute/memory**, so TPVFormer uses a tri-perspective view to factor $\mathcal{O}(XYZ)\to\mathcal{O}(XY+YZ+ZX)$ as an approximation of dense voxels. Tesla's 2022 AI Day made the Occupancy Network the core of its production vision stack, pushing this route into the industrial mainstream. It can be seen as a 3D generalization of the BEV in §2: BEV is an occupancy map with z flattened; the occupancy network keeps z.

## 5. Multi-Sensor Fusion

No single sensor is omnipotent. The goal of fusion is to **complement strengths and provide redundancy** (safety redundancy is a hard requirement for production).

### 5.1 Three Fusion Levels

| Fusion level | Where it fuses | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Early (data-level)** | raw-data level (e.g. paint LiDAR points with image color, or decorate the cloud with image features) | minimal information loss, strong complementarity | needs precise spatio-temporal calibration & sync, sensitive to calibration error |
| **Mid (feature-level)** | after each modality is encoded into features, align & concatenate in BEV/voxel space (BEVFusion-style) | balances expressiveness and robustness, currently mainstream | needs a unified representation space, engineering-complex |
| **Late (object-level)** | each modality emits detections, then box-level association / Kalman fusion | decoupled modules, easy to add sensors, graceful degradation if one path fails | loses info early; weak targets missed by all paths cannot be recovered |

Trend: a **BEV feature-fusion** backbone (BEVFusion unifies camera LSS features and LiDAR voxel features in the same BEV plane before concatenation), backstopped by late fusion for safety redundancy.

### 5.2 Camera + LiDAR + Radar Complementarity

| Dimension | Camera | LiDAR | Radar (mmWave) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Depth/ranging | weak (monocular ill-posed) | **strong** (direct cm-level) | medium (m-level, still OK at range) |
| Semantics/color/texture | **strong** (reads lights, signs, lanes) | weak (no color) | none |
| Angular resolution | high | high | low (hard to separate adjacent targets) |
| Direct velocity | none (needs multiple frames) | none (needs multiple frames) | **strong** (Doppler gives radial velocity directly) |
| Adverse weather (rain/fog/dust) | poor | medium (rain/fog scatter & attenuate) | **strong** (good penetration) |
| Night/glare | poor (light-dependent) | **strong** (active light) | strong |
| Cost | **low** | high | low |

The complementarity is clear: cameras give semantics, radar gives velocity and all-weather penetration, LiDAR gives precise geometry. Once aligned in BEV space, each one's weakness is covered by the others; if any path fails (camera glare, LiDAR in rain/fog), the system can still run degraded — exactly the value of late-fusion redundancy.

## 6. HD Map vs Mapless

### 6.1 HD Map: A Prior as "Offline Perception"

An HD map stores, offline at **lane-level** (centimeter) precision, lane centerlines, connection topology, stop lines, crosswalks, speed limits, and traffic-light positions. It is essentially **a portion of perception done offline and cached**: with a map, online work is only localization + dynamic-obstacle perception, while static semantics are looked up — greatly reducing online load and improving stability.

The cost is **heaviness**: collection and high-precision labeling are expensive; maps go stale (construction, re-routing) and need a vast update loop; and they strongly depend on cm-level localization to align the map to the current frame. Coverage thus becomes a scaling bottleneck — which directly spawned the mapless route.

### 6.2 Mapless / Online Mapping: MapTR-style

"Mapless" does not mean no map but **building a local vectorized map online in real time** (without a pre-stored HD map). **MapTR** (Liao et al., ICLR 2023) frames online mapping as **set prediction of vectorized map elements**: a set of DETR-style queries directly outputs **ordered point-sequence polylines** for lane lines, boundaries, crosswalks, etc., rather than segmenting first and post-process vectorizing.

Technical point: map elements are **permutation-equivariant** (a polyline read forward or backward, a closed polygon from any start point are equivalent). MapTR models this symmetry with **hierarchical bipartite matching at the point-set level**, making training stable and inference end-to-end vectorized. The resulting vector map directly serves planning as lane constraints.

Trade-off: HD map = heavy prior, stable, limited coverage; mapless = light, scalable, but online-mapping quality at complex intersections still trails carefully curated offline maps. Production often compromises — a lightweight "navigation-grade" map (SD map) on main roads + online mapping to fill in lane-level detail.

## 7. Localization

Localization must produce a 6-DoF ego pose in the map/world frame; production requires **cm-level, high-rate, uninterrupted** output. No single method satisfies all of these, so a **complementary filter / factor graph** fuses them.

### 7.1 GNSS + IMU Integrated Navigation

- **GNSS** (GPS/BeiDou, with RTK differential reaching cm-level) gives **global absolute** position, but is **low-rate** (~10 Hz), loses lock with urban-canyon multipath and tunnels, and can jump.
- **IMU** (accelerometers + gyros) is **high-rate** (>100 Hz) and short-term accurate, but its integration **drifts** (error accumulates over time) and is unusable alone.

The two are naturally complementary: GNSS corrects the IMU's long-term drift, while the IMU does **dead reckoning** smoothing during GNSS dropout/low-rate gaps. Fusion uses an **error-state Kalman filter (ESKF)** or graph optimization, often with wheel odometry added. This is the same family of state estimation as in [SLAM](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/SLAM.en.md) (see its EKF/factor-graph chapters).

### 7.2 LiDAR / Visual SLAM Registration

Where GNSS is lost (tunnels, garages, urban canyons), localization relies on **map registration**:

- **LiDAR registration**: register the current cloud to a pre-built point-cloud map via **ICP / NDT** to solve for pose. Geometrically precise and illumination-robust, it is the workhorse of high-precision localization (NDT builds the map as a normal-distribution grid, faster and more stable than point-wise ICP).
- **Visual localization**: use [ORB-SLAM](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/SLAM.en.md)-style feature SLAM or visual relocalization to match the current frame to a visual map. Cheap, but sensitive to illumination and texture-poor scenes.

Note the distinction between two related but different problems: **SLAM** maps and localizes simultaneously when no map exists (the exploration phase); production driving **localization** is mostly **registration/relocalization under a known map** (the map is already stored). This note goes only this deep into localization; the probabilistic basis of state estimation, loop closure, and pose-graph optimization are in [SLAM: Simultaneous Localization and Mapping](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/SLAM.en.md).

## Method Comparison

| Dimension | Camera 3D (LSS/BEVFormer) | LiDAR (PointPillars/CenterPoint) | Occupancy Network | Camera+LiDAR fusion (BEVFusion) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Geometric accuracy | medium (relies on learned depth) | **high** (direct measurement) | medium-high | **high** |
| Semantic richness | **high** | low | medium (semantic voxels) | **high** |
| Long-tail/irregular targets | weak | medium | **strong** (class-agnostic) | medium-strong |
| Cost | **low** | high | follows input sensors | high |
| Compute | medium | medium (PointPillars fast) | **high** (dense voxels) | high |
| Calibration/sync sensitivity | low (single cam) | low | low | **high** (multi-modal alignment) |
| All-weather robustness | weak | medium | follows input | strong (stronger with radar) |
| Representatives | BEVFormer, LSS, BEVDepth | CenterPoint | OccNet, TPVFormer | BEVFusion |

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Treating monocular 3D depth as reliable ground truth**: monocular depth is a learned prior with large systematic bias under rain/fog, domain shift, and unseen object sizes; safety-critical components should not rely on monocular depth alone.
2. **Ignoring BEV's calibration/time-sync dependence**: all "project to one BEV" fusion assumes accurate extrinsics calibration and multi-sensor timestamps; calibration drift directly misaligns boxes — the most insidious production accident source.
3. **Using only 3D boxes, ignoring long-tail geometry**: irregular/unseen-class obstacles get missed by box models; occupancy grids exist precisely for this (see §4).
4. **High mAP ≠ road safety**: detection mAP is an open-loop metric; recall on small/distant/occluded safety-critical targets and velocity-estimation error often matter more than mean mAP; nuScenes NDS (including velocity/attribute error) or closed-loop evaluation is more reliable.
5. **Confusing SLAM with localization**: production localization is mostly "registration under a known map", not SLAM from scratch; conflating the two overestimates the reliability requirements of online mapping.
6. **Over-relying on the HD map**: when the map is stale (construction re-routing), it is dangerous without online perception as a backstop; the map is always a prior and cannot replace real-time perception.

## Frontier Trends

1. **Unifying BEV / occupancy as a general world model**: perception output shifts from "a set of boxes" to a "dense BEV/occupancy world model", shared by downstream prediction, planning, and end-to-end (UniAD); see the [paradigm taxonomy](../01_Foundations/自动驾驶范式与方法谱系.en.md).
2. **Going mapless and light-map**: MapTR-style online vectorized mapping + SD-map prior reduces dependence on heavy HD maps for scalability.
3. **Occupancy flow and 4D occupancy**: adding occupancy flow (per-voxel velocity) to occupancy voxels extends "what/where" to "where it moves", blurring the perception–prediction boundary.
4. **Vision-first, radar revival**: pure-vision occupancy (Tesla) cuts cost, while 4D imaging radar (with height/angular resolution) returns to the fusion stack to fix the all-weather gap.
5. **Joint differentiable perception-prediction-planning**: detection/tracking/occupancy are no longer optimized in isolation but back-propagated under the final planning objective (UniAD), easing the information bottleneck of intermediate representations.
