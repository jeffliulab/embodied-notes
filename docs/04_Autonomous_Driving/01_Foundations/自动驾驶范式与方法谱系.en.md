# Autonomous Driving Paradigms & Method Taxonomy

> *Separate two things — the "perception–prediction–planning–control" pipeline, and "what method produces the decision" — and the autonomous-driving method landscape becomes clear.*
>
> **Difficulty**: Intermediate

## 1. Why We Need a Map

ALVINN, PilotNet, Apollo, ChauffeurNet, UniAD, Tesla FSD... the sheer number of "method" names in autonomous driving is dizzying, and beginners easily blend them into one mush: thinking "end-to-end" means "uses a neural network," that "imitation learning" means "end-to-end," that "modular" means "the old non-learning approach."

Almost all of this confusion comes from **cramming two orthogonal things onto one axis**. Untangling autonomous driving needs only two coordinate axes:

- **Architecture axis**: from a fully decoupled **modular pipeline**, to an **end-to-end (E2E)** single network. It answers "how many blocks the system has, and how information flows."
- **Learning/decision axis**: from **rules + optimal control**, to **imitation learning (BC/IL)**, **reinforcement learning (RL)**, **world model + MPC**. It answers "where the decision comes from, and what trains it."

This note does not dig into any single system; it draws this 2-D map and names the representatives in each cell. By the end you should be able to say "UniAD is end-to-end-differentiable modular + imitation learning" rather than vaguely "it's end-to-end."

## 2. First Axis: System Architecture (Modular Pipeline ↔ End-to-End E2E)

### 2.1 Modular Pipeline: Perception → Prediction → Planning → Control

The classic AD stack **decouples** the task into modules connected by **human-defined intermediate representations**:

- **Perception**: outputs detection boxes, lane lines, drivable area, occupancy grid;
- **Prediction**: outputs future trajectories of other agents;
- **Planning**: outputs the ego target trajectory / action sequence;
- **Control**: turns the trajectory into steering / throttle / brake signals.

Its strengths are **interpretability, debuggability, division of labor** — a faulty module can be isolated and replaced, and each module can be trained or validated independently against its own ground truth. Apollo and Autoware are typical. The cost is that the intermediate representation is an **information bottleneck**: information dropped by perception is gone, and locally optimizing each module is not globally optimal.

### 2.2 End-to-End E2E: Sensor → Control, One Network All the Way

End-to-end collapses the whole chain into **one (differentiable) network**, mapping raw sensor input directly to control (or a trajectory). Strengths: **joint optimization** (gradients flow from the control end all the way back to perception, aligning the whole system to one objective) and **no human information bottleneck**. Costs: **black-box, hard to verify, data-hungry**, and — central to this section — it exposes imitation learning's [covariate shift](../05_Learning_E2E/协变量偏移与闭环鸿沟.en.md) problem most completely.

### 2.3 The Middle Ground: End-to-End-Differentiable Modular

The most common misconception is to equate "end-to-end" with "no modules." **It is not.** The essence of end-to-end is that **gradients can flow from the final objective all the way to the front end**, not that "the network has no structure."

UniAD (CVPR 2023 Best Paper) is the exemplar: it **keeps** the semantics of perception, prediction, and planning modules (each even has its own intermediate supervision), yet strings them into a **differentiable whole** that back-propagates from the final planning objective — so it is both "modular" (clear module boundaries) and "end-to-end" (gradients flow through). Architecture is really a **continuous spectrum**, not a binary choice.

## 3. Second Axis: Learning/Decision Paradigm

Under the same architecture, decisions can be produced by completely different methods:

- **3.1 Rules + optimal control**: does not "learn" a policy; relies on engineered finite-state machines, cost functions, and optimal control ([MPC](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/MPC.en.md), LQR). Provable and interpretable, but the long tail is handled by hand-written rules.
- **3.2 Imitation learning (IL / BC)**: supervised fitting of the expert's (observation → action) mapping, no reward function needed. Easiest to start with, but has the structural [covariate shift](../05_Learning_E2E/协变量偏移与闭环鸿沟.en.md) flaw — the core problem of this section.
- **3.3 Reinforcement learning (RL)**: trial-and-error with reward in simulation/closed loop. Mostly used in the planning/control layer, or as a training signal for an end-to-end policy; online trial-and-error on a real car is unsafe, so it is usually done in simulation.
- **3.4 World model + MPC (model-based)**: first learn environment dynamics (predict future observations/states), then roll out and plan in "imagination." Representatives are Wayve's MILE and GAIA-1; see [Representation & World Models](../../00_Robotics/01_Embodiment_Theory/表征与世界模型.en.md).

## 4. The Crux of Orthogonality: BC ≠ E2E

This is the single most important takeaway:

> **BC (behavior cloning) is a learning method** — supervised fitting of the expert's (observation → action).
> **End-to-end E2E is an architecture** — sensor to control, one network all the way.
> The two lie on **different axes** and can be combined freely.

PilotNet = BC **and** E2E (supervised regression from raw pixels to steering); but you can also **do BC on high-level discrete actions/trajectories without being end-to-end at all** (supervising a modular intermediate representation, e.g., cloning trajectories on a BEV). Hence **BC ≠ E2E**.

### 4.2 Three Common Confusions, Cleared Up

1. **"End-to-end" ≠ "no modules"**: UniAD keeps perception/prediction/planning modules but gradients flow through, so it is still end-to-end (see §2.3).
2. **"Uses a neural network" ≠ "end-to-end"**: every module in a modular stack can be an NN (CNN for perception, Transformer for prediction), but interfaces are manually severed, gradients do not flow through, so it is not end-to-end.
3. **"BC" ≠ "must start from pixels"**: you can do BC at any abstraction level — on raw pixels (PilotNet), or on a BEV intermediate representation, on high-level discrete actions or trajectories (ChauffeurNet's mid-to-mid, Learning by Cheating's privileged-student trajectory cloning).

## 5. The 2-D Taxonomy Matrix (Core Deliverable)

Crossing the two axes, AD's representative systems fall into place:

| Architecture ＼ Learning | Rules + optimal control | Imitation learning (BC/IL) | Reinforcement learning (RL) | World model + MPC |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Modular pipeline** | Apollo / Autoware classic stack | Learning by Cheating (privileged teacher), ChauffeurNet (mid-to-mid) | RL in planning/control layer (Apollo RL module) | modular prediction + MPC planning |
| **End-to-end E2E** | (rare — pure-optimization E2E is uncommon) | ALVINN, PilotNet, TransFuser, VAD | end-to-end RL (Roach on CARLA, etc.) | MILE, GAIA-1 (generative world models) |
| **E2E-differentiable modular** | — | UniAD (CVPR 2023 Best Paper), VAD | UniAD + RL fine-tune | differentiable world model + planning |

> The table deliberately gives "E2E-differentiable modular" its own row, because UniAD / VAD are both "end-to-end" (gradients flow) and "keep module semantics" — reconfirming architecture is a continuous spectrum, not binary.

## 7. Method Comparison

| Dimension | Modular + rule stack | Modular IL | End-to-end IL | End-to-end RL | World model |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Interpretability | high | medium | low | low | medium |
| Data demand | low (rules) | medium | high | medium (sim) | high |
| Long-tail handling | poor (hand rules) | medium | medium | better | good (can synthesize) |
| Verification difficulty | low | medium | high | high | high |
| Needs closed-loop sim | no | recommended | recommended | required | recommended |
| Covariate-shift sensitivity | low | high | **highest** | low (closed-loop training) | medium |
| Representative | Apollo | ChauffeurNet | PilotNet/TransFuser | Roach | MILE/GAIA-1 |

## 8. Common Pitfalls

1. **Treating BC and E2E as synonyms**: the number-one myth this note exists to dispel (see §4).
2. **Assuming end-to-end means no intermediate representation**: differentiable modular (UniAD) is the counterexample.
3. **Assuming modular must be non-differentiable / non-NN**: each module in a modular stack can be an NN.
4. **Ignoring "good offline metric ≠ good on-road closed-loop"**: selecting a model purely by open-loop L2 gets bitten by covariate shift — see [Covariate Shift & the Closed-Loop Gap](../05_Learning_E2E/协变量偏移与闭环鸿沟.en.md).
5. **Assuming pure end-to-end must be better**: the cost of joint optimization is data hunger, hard verification, and the worst covariate shift.

---

## 9. Frontier Trends

1. **End-to-end differentiability**: after UniAD, end-to-end while keeping interpretable module semantics is the mainstream direction.
2. **Generative world models for the long tail**: GAIA-1 and others synthesize rare dangerous scenarios, mitigating data scarcity and covariate shift.
3. **VLA / large models entering driving**: driving-flavored vision-language-action models bring language-level interpretable planning; see [VLA Models](../../02_Robot_Learning/01_Foundations/VLA模型.en.md).
4. **Closed-loop simulation evaluation becomes standard**: nuPlan, CARLA Leaderboard make closed-loop success/violation an official metric; see [Datasets & Closed-Loop Evaluation](../06_Data_Eval/数据集与闭环评测.en.md).
