## 1. What Is Autonomous Driving (Task Definition)

Autonomous driving (AD) solves a **partially observable, stochastic, multi-agent, safety-critical** sequential decision problem: based on onboard-sensor observations of its surroundings, the vehicle must — subject to traffic rules, comfort, and safety constraints — **continuously** output steering, throttle, and brake to deliver itself safely from start to destination.

Formally, view it as a POMDP (Partially Observable Markov Decision Process) $\langle \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{O}, T, Z, R \rangle$:

- **State** $s_t \in \mathcal{S}$: ego pose and velocity, plus the positions/intents of all other traffic participants, road topology, signal lights — most of which is **not directly observable**.
- **Observation** $o_t \in \mathcal{O}$: camera pixels, LiDAR point clouds, radar returns, IMU/GNSS readings — noisy, occluded, partial.
- **Action** $a_t \in \mathcal{A}$: chassis controls (steering angle $\delta$, longitudinal acceleration $a$), or a target trajectory one layer up.
- **Objective**: maximize expected return $\mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_t \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t)\right]$, with the reward encoding "arrive + obey rules + avoid collision + ride smoothly."

The problem is hard for three reasons: (1) **the long tail** — 99.9% of miles are uneventful, yet crashes happen in extremely rare corner cases; (2) **closed-loop coupling** — the car's actions change the world it sees next, so good offline metrics do not imply good on-road behavior (see [Covariate Shift & the Closed-Loop Gap](../05_Learning_E2E/协变量偏移与闭环鸿沟.en.md)); (3) **safety-criticality** — failure can cost lives, leaving a fault tolerance far tighter than for a recommender or chatbot.

## 2. SAE L0–L5 Automation Levels

The industry-standard levels come from SAE J3016. There is essentially one dividing line: **who monitors the driving environment, and who falls back on failure.** L0–L2 are "driver drives, system assists," with the human always liable; from L3 the system becomes the driving agent within its design domain; L4/L5 require **no human takeover** within the domain.

| Level | Name | Who monitors | Fallback | Typical function / representative |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **L0** | No automation | Driver | Driver | AEB emergency braking, blind-spot warning (alert/momentary only) |
| **L1** | Driver assistance | Driver | Driver | ACC **or** lane keeping (one axis, pick one) |
| **L2** | Partial automation | Driver | Driver | ACC + lane centering **simultaneously** (lat + long); Tesla Autopilot/FSD (Supervised), various NOA / highway pilot |
| **L3** | Conditional automation | **System** (in-domain) | Driver (must take over within seconds) | hands/eyes-off in domain; Mercedes Drive Pilot, Honda Legend |
| **L4** | High automation | **System** (in-domain) | **System** (in-domain MRC, minimal-risk state) | driverless in domain; Waymo One, Cruise (suspended), Baidu Apollo Go |
| **L5** | Full automation | **System** (any domain) | **System** | driverless on any road — no production system exists |

> **L2 assistance vs L4 driverless: the essential difference is not "capability" but "liability and fallback."**
> However smart an L2 system is, the human is **legally required to monitor continuously and take over at any moment** — it is "advanced cruise." Within its Operational Design Domain (ODD), an L4 system is **responsible for its own safety** and executes a minimal-risk maneuver (pulling over) on failure, with no safety driver needed. L3 is the awkward in-between: the human may go hands-off, but must take over within seconds when prompted, and "takeover handoff" is a recognized human-factors hazard — so many companies skip L3 and go straight for L4.

<p class="figure-caption">Figure 1 — The four-layer autonomous-driving pipeline: raw sensor data is abstracted by perception into detections/BEV, prediction yields others' future trajectories, planning solves for the ego target trajectory, and control tracks it into steering/throttle/brake. The "intermediate representations" labeled on the arrows are the human-defined interfaces between modules — also the source of the modular stack's greatest virtue (interpretability) and its cost (the information bottleneck).</p>

### 3.1 Perception

Turns raw sensor data into a structured description of the world: 3D object detection and tracking, lane / drivable-area segmentation, occupancy grids, and ego localization (SLAM / HD-map matching). The modern mainstream projects multi-camera / multi-modal features into a unified **bird's-eye view (BEV)** space — LSS (Lift-Splat-Shoot) does the geometric lift, BEVFormer uses spatiotemporal attention — and then detects and segments in BEV; on the LiDAR side there are VoxelNet / PointPillars / CenterPoint.

### 3.2 Prediction (Motion Forecasting)

Outputs a **multimodal** trajectory distribution for surrounding traffic participants over the next 3–8 seconds (a car at an intersection might go straight or turn right, so multiple hypotheses with probabilities must be emitted). Representative methods encode the map and history into a vectorized graph (VectorNet, LaneGCN), or output multiple modes via a CVAE (Trajectron++).

### 3.3 Planning

In the predicted dynamic environment, solves for a **safe, rule-compliant, comfortable** target trajectory for the ego. Common practice: behavioral decision-making (a finite-state machine for lane changes / yielding) + trajectory optimization (lattice sampling or constrained optimization in Frenet coordinates), encoding collision, comfort, and progress into a cost function.

### 3.4 Control

**Tracks** the planned trajectory into chassis signals. Laterally, Pure Pursuit, the Stanley controller, or [MPC](../../00_Robotics/02_Classical_Robotics/MPC.en.md) rolling out on a vehicle-dynamics model; longitudinally, PID/MPC for speed tracking. Control runs at the highest rate (50–100 Hz) and has the strictest real-time and stability demands.

## 4. Sensor Suite and Fusion

No single sensor covers all conditions, so both production and Robotaxi rely on a **complementary, redundant** multi-sensor suite. The pros and cons roughly break down as:

| Sensor | Measures | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Camera** | color/texture/semantics (passive light) | high resolution, reads lights/signs/lanes, cheap | no direct range, poor in glare/night/rain/fog, needs compute to recover 3D |
| **LiDAR** | precise 3D point cloud (active laser) | direct, dense, accurate range and geometry, unaffected at night | expensive, attenuated by rain/snow/fog, points carry no semantics, motion distortion |
| **radar (mmWave)** | range + radial velocity (Doppler) | direct velocity, penetrates rain/fog/dust, long range, cheap, all-weather | low angular resolution, multipath clutter, hard to resolve stationary objects |
| **IMU** | angular rate + acceleration | high rate (hundreds of Hz), no external dependence, accurate short-term | error drifts as it integrates over time, must be corrected by other sources |
| **GNSS (GPS etc.)** | global absolute position | global reference, no long-term drift | low rate, loses lock in urban canyons/tunnels, meter-level (needs RTK/IMU fusion) |

The complementarity is clear: cameras give semantics, LiDAR/radar give geometry and velocity, IMU+GNSS give localization; radar covers the bad-weather gaps of camera/LiDAR all-weather. A classic debate is **vision-only** (Tesla — cameras + neural nets to estimate depth, saving cost) vs **multi-modal** (Waymo et al. — camera + LiDAR + radar, trading physical ranging for redundant safety).

### 4.1 Early Fusion vs Late Fusion

- **Late fusion (object-level)**: each sensor runs its own full perception, emits its own object list, and these are associated and Kalman-fused at the object level. **Modules are decoupled, easy to engineer, and single-source faults are isolable**, but raw information is discarded early, so a weak target may already be filtered out within a single source.
- **Early fusion (feature-level)**: aligns and fuses modalities at the raw-data or feature level (e.g., projecting LiDAR points onto the image, or concatenating camera and point-cloud features in BEV), letting the network jointly exploit all information. **It uses information most fully and is more sensitive to weak targets**, but demands extreme calibration / time synchronization and is more of a black box. Modern BEV approaches (the BEVFusion family) mostly take early / feature-level fusion.

The trade-off is isomorphic to "modular vs end-to-end": late fusion is like modular (clear interfaces, interpretable), early fusion is like end-to-end (joint optimization, no information bottleneck).

## 5. Modular vs End-to-End Trade-offs

The four-layer pipeline of §3 is synonymous with the **modular** paradigm: modules are joined by human-defined intermediate representations — interpretable, divisible across teams, separately verifiable — at the cost that the intermediate representation is an information bottleneck and per-module local optima do not equal a global optimum. **End-to-end (E2E)** instead compresses the whole chain into one differentiable network from sensors directly to control/trajectory — jointly optimized, with no human bottleneck — but a black box that is hard to verify, data-hungry, and exposes imitation learning's [covariate shift](../05_Learning_E2E/协变量偏移与闭环鸿沟.en.md) most fully.

A key clarification: **"end-to-end" ≠ "no modules"** — UniAD (CVPR 2023 Best Paper) keeps the semantics of perception/prediction/planning modules but lets gradients backpropagate from the planning objective all the way to perception, making it "differentiable modular end-to-end"; architecture is really a **continuous spectrum**, not a binary. How this axis — and the orthogonal "learning method (rules/IL/RL/world model)" axis — cross is covered in [Autonomous Driving Paradigms & Method Taxonomy](自动驾驶范式与方法谱系.en.md).

## 6. Robotaxi vs Production Driver Assistance

The two industrial routes differ sharply in goal, constraints, and business model:

| Dimension | Robotaxi (L4) | Production assistance (L2/L2+) |
|---|---|---|
| Target level | L4, no safety driver onboard | L2/L2+, human always monitoring |
| Liable party | Operator/system | Driver |
| ODD | fenced urban area, HD map + remote monitoring | highway/urban pilot, but human falls back |
| Sensors | heavy suite (multi-LiDAR + cameras + radar), cost amortized over operations | cost-sensitive, trending vision-only / light LiDAR |
| Safety redundancy | hardware-level redundancy (dual power/steering/compute) + MRC | the human is the final redundancy |
| Business model | per-mile mobility service | car sales / software subscription |
| Representatives | Waymo, Baidu Apollo Go, WeRide/Pony.ai | Tesla FSD (Supervised), various highway/urban NOA |

The **Operational Design Domain (ODD)** is L4's linchpin: it explicitly bounds where the system "is guaranteed to work" — geofence, weather, time of day, road type, speed cap. L4 is not "can do anything," but "is responsible for itself within the ODD, and refuses the task or executes an MRC beyond it."

**Safety redundancy**: because L4 has no human fallback, it must be **fail-operational** — after any critical component fails, the system can still bring the car safely to a minimal-risk state (MRC), hence dual power, dual steering, dual compute, and cross-checked multi-sensor inputs. L2 treats the driver as the final redundancy and only needs to be **fail-safe** (alert and disengage). This difference in redundancy level is one root cause of a Robotaxi's per-vehicle cost vastly exceeding a production car's.

## 7. A Brief History

- **2004–2007 · DARPA Challenges**: the inaugural 2004 Grand Challenge (240 km across the Mojave) saw no vehicle finish; in 2005, Stanford's "Stanley" (Thrun's team) won, and its Stanley lateral controller and probabilistic mapping became classics; the 2007 Urban Challenge moved to a city road network (with traffic rules and other-vehicle interaction), won by CMU's "Boss." These three races shaped the prototype of the modern modular stack and trained the core talent of the later Waymo/Cruise/Aurora.
- **2009– · Waymo (formerly the Google self-driving project)**: starting from the DARPA cohort, it long bet on **HD maps + multi-LiDAR + a modular stack**, launching the safety-driver-free Waymo One in Phoenix in 2020 — the benchmark for L4 Robotaxi.
- **2014– · Tesla Autopilot → FSD**: starting from Mobileye vision-based ACC/LKA, it turned to a **vision-only + shadow-mode crowdsourced-data** production-L2 route; from FSD v12 (2023–2024) it made planning/control heavily **end-to-end** ("photon in, control out"), epitomizing the industry's shift from the modular stack toward end-to-end learning.
- **2020s · the end-to-end turn in academia and industry**: UniAD (CVPR 2023 Best Paper) pushed differentiable modular end-to-end into the mainstream, VAD made it vectorized and efficient, Wayve's MILE/GAIA-1 introduced world models, and nuPlan/CARLA established closed-loop evaluation as standard — the pendulum swung from 1980s end-to-end (ALVINN) to 2010s modular, then fused both in the 2020s with "differentiable modular + world models."

## Method Comparison

| Dimension | Modular + rule-based stack | End-to-end learned stack |
|---|---|---|
| Information flow | human-defined intermediate reps (boxes/BEV/trajectory) | differentiable features, gradients flow through |
| Interpretability | high, faults localizable per module | low, black box |
| Verification / debug | easy, each module has ground truth | hard, needs closed-loop evaluation |
| Data demand | low (relies on rules and per-module labels) | high (massive driving data) |
| Long tail / corner cases | piled-up hand-written rules, brittle | data-driven, high potential but hard to guarantee |
| Global optimality | per-module local optima | jointly optimized |
| Covariate-shift sensitivity | low | high (open-loop training, on-road shift) |
| Representatives | Apollo, Autoware, classic Waymo stack | UniAD, VAD, Tesla FSD v12 |

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Treating SAE levels as a capability scale rather than a liability scale**: the real divide between L2 and L4 is "who falls back," not "who is smarter" — a very capable L2 still legally requires the human to monitor throughout.
2. **Assuming L5 already exists**: no production L5 exists; even L4 only operates within a fenced ODD.
3. **Conflating "end-to-end" with "no modules"**: UniAD is the counterexample — end-to-end is about whether gradients flow through, not whether modules exist (see §5).
4. **Ignoring the ODD**: judging an L4 system out of context of its operational design domain is wrong; a "failure" beyond the ODD is a design boundary, not a bug.
5. **Selecting systems by offline metrics alone**: high open-loop detection/prediction mAP ≠ safe closed-loop driving; closed-loop coupling and [covariate shift](../05_Learning_E2E/协变量偏移与闭环鸿沟.en.md) bite back.
6. **Over-mythologizing vision-only or LiDAR-only**: sensors are an engineering trade-off (cost vs redundancy), not a creed; multi-modal early fusion depends on rigorous calibration and time synchronization.

---

## Frontier Trends

1. **Differentiable end-to-end becomes mainstream**: after UniAD, architectures that keep interpretable module semantics yet let gradients flow through are a shared direction for research and industry.
2. **Occupancy grids and world models**: replacing "detect only known classes" with a general 3D occupancy/flow representation, and using generative world models (GAIA-1) to synthesize rare, dangerous scenarios to fill the long tail.
3. **VLA / large models entering driving**: vision-language-action models bring language-level, interpretable high-level decisions and common-sense generalization to unseen scenes.
4. **Standardizing closed-loop simulation evaluation**: nuPlan and the CARLA Leaderboard make closed-loop success rate / violation score formal metrics, replacing open-loop mAP alone.
5. **Production end-to-end deployment**: the end-to-end turn from Tesla FSD v12 onward is pushing the industry toward "massive real driving data + end-to-end learning" as a scalable product route.
