    Motion Prediction

    Motion Prediction — given another agent's past trajectory and the HD map, forecast how it might move over the next few seconds; the hard part is not "predicting one line" but that the future is inherently multi-modal.

    Difficulty: Intermediate Prerequisites: Autonomous Driving Paradigms & Method Taxonomy Further reading: Planning & Decision Making

    1. Why Predict Other Agents (Planning Needs the Future)

    Planning is fundamentally about making decisions over the future: whether the ego should merge, whether to yield at an intersection, how much following distance to keep — all depend on what surrounding cars, pedestrians, and cyclists will do next. With only a snapshot of the present (position + velocity), the planner either treats everyone as moving in a straight line at constant speed (the constant-velocity model) or treats every possibility as an obstacle and becomes overly conservative. The former gets you T-boned by a left-turning car; the latter "freezes" in place and cannot move — the freezing robot problem.

    Prediction is therefore the bridge between perception and planning: perception tells you "who is on the scene, where, and heading which way," prediction extrapolates these states into "a distribution over the possible trajectories of each agent over the next 3–8 s," and planning then makes safe and efficient decisions over that future distribution.

    Prediction is a distinct hard problem for three essential reasons:

    • Multi-agent: dozens of traffic participants may be present, and their intents are coupled (I only dare to go once you let me).
    • Multi-modal: the same car at the same instant might go straight or turn right — these are two mutually exclusive discrete intents, not noise along a single trajectory.
    • Strong map constraints: vehicles almost always follow lanes; feasible futures are tightly constrained by the road topology. Feeding the map into the model is the single biggest gain modern predictors have over early LSTM extrapolation.

    2. Problem Formalization

    Let there be \(N\) agents on the scene. The state sequence of agent \(i\) over the past \(T\) steps is

    \[ \mathbf{s}^{(i)}_{-T:0} = \big(s^{(i)}_{-T}, \dots, s^{(i)}_{0}\big),\quad s^{(i)}_t \in \mathbb{R}^d \]

    where \(d\) typically includes position, heading, velocity, etc. Given scene context \(\mathcal{C}\) (HD-map lane polylines, stop lines, traffic-light states, the relations among all agents), the goal is to estimate the conditional distribution over the future \(H\) steps:

    \[ p\big(\mathbf{Y}^{(i)}_{1:H} \mid \mathbf{s}^{(1:N)}_{-T:0},\, \mathcal{C}\big),\qquad \mathbf{Y}^{(i)}_{1:H} = (y^{(i)}_1, \dots, y^{(i)}_H),\; y^{(i)}_h \in \mathbb{R}^2 . \]

    The crucial point is that the output is a distribution, not a single trajectory. Practical systems usually discretize it into \(K\) modes, each with a trajectory \(\hat{Y}^{(i,k)}\) and a probability \(\pi^{(i,k)}\):

    \[ p(\mathbf{Y}^{(i)}) \approx \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi^{(i,k)}\, \mathcal{N}\big(\mathbf{Y}^{(i)} \mid \hat{Y}^{(i,k)}, \Sigma^{(i,k)}\big),\qquad \sum_k \pi^{(i,k)} = 1 . \]

    Typical setup: sampling at \(10\,\text{Hz}\), horizon \(H\) corresponding to \(3\text{s}\) (Argoverse) up to \(6\text{s}\) (Waymo / nuScenes), with \(K=6\) modes (the official setting on the Argoverse / Waymo leaderboards).

    Joint vs marginal prediction: the equation above outputs a distribution for each \(i\) independently — this is marginal prediction, and it can produce contradictory combinations like "both cars predict they will occupy the same gap." Joint prediction directly models \(p(\mathbf{Y}^{(1:N)})\), guaranteeing scene-level consistency; it is harder but far more useful for planning (this is exactly what Waymo's Interaction / Sim Agents tracks test).

    3. Scene Representation

    To encode "history + map + multi-agent relations" into a network, there are two mainstream routes.

    3.1 Rasterized BEV

    Render the scene into a multi-channel bird's-eye-view (BEV) bitmap: one channel for lanes, one for crosswalks, several channels stacking each agent's past positions as motion-history frames, then extract features with a CNN. Early examples include ChauffeurNet, IntentNet, and MultiPath.

    • Pros: simple to implement, directly reuses mature CNNs; spatial relations among elements are naturally aligned.
    • Cons: resolution vs receptive field is a deadlock — too coarse loses detail, too fine blows up compute; sparse, vectorial, directional structures like lanes are crammed into dense pixels, which is extremely wasteful; long-range topological relations have to propagate through very deep convolutions.

    3.2 Vectorized: VectorNet / LaneGCN

    Represent both map and trajectories as polylines: a lane centerline is a sequence of directed segments, and a history trajectory is also a sequence of segments. Each polyline is first aggregated into a node feature by a subgraph, then a global graph network lets all polylines (lanes, agents) interact. This is the core idea of VectorNet (Gao et al. 2020) — "represent with a graph, not pixels."

    LaneGCN (Liang et al. 2020) goes further, modeling four connection types specifically for the lane graph:

    • AL / LA (agent↔lane): the association between agents and nearby lanes;
    • LL (lane↔lane): lane topology (predecessor/successor/left-right adjacency), propagated along the lane network with graph convolutions;
    • AA (agent↔agent): interactions among agents.

    Benefits of vectorization: precise (no geometry lost), sparse (only real elements are computed), with built-in direction and topology semantics. The cost is more engineering complexity and greater sensitivity to map quality. Today's leaderboard mainstays (LaneGCN, TNT, DenseTNT, HiVT, Wayformer, MTR) are almost all vectorized + Transformer/GNN.

    Rasterized BEV Vectorized polyline
    Geometric precision limited by resolution exact
    Compute scaling \(\propto\) pixel area \(\propto\) #elements (sparse)
    Encoder CNN GNN / Transformer
    Map topology implicit (via conv) explicit (lane-graph edges)
    Examples ChauffeurNet, MultiPath VectorNet, LaneGCN

    4. Multi-Modality & Uncertainty

    4.1 Why a Single Trajectory Is Not Enough

    Future uncertainty has two essentially different sources:

    • Aleatoric / intra-mode: continuous noise under a fixed intent — going straight, speed can be faster or slower, with lateral jitter. Describable by a per-timestep Gaussian covariance \(\Sigma_h\).
    • Epistemic / inter-mode (intent): discrete, mutually exclusive intents — straight vs right turn. This cannot be captured by one Gaussian: the "average" of the two trajectories lands on the curb, a physically impossible path.

    This is the fatal flaw of regressing a single trajectory (minimizing MSE): the optimal MSE solution is the conditional expectation \(\mathbb{E}[Y]\), which on a bimodal distribution equals the average of the two peaks — a "fence-sitting" trajectory resembling neither and possibly hitting a wall (mode averaging / mode collapse). So prediction must output multiple modes.

    4.2 Mixture Density Networks (MDN)

    Have the network directly output \(K\) tuples \((\pi_k, \mu_k, \Sigma_k)\), trained with negative log-likelihood. The issue is mode collapse: modes collapse together, or some modes never receive gradient. Tricks like winner-takes-all (back-propagating the trajectory loss only through the mode closest to ground truth) are needed to stabilize training.

    4.3 Anchors / Goal Conditioning: MultiPath → TNT → DenseTNT

    Decompose "predict a curve" into first pick a goal point, then regress a path to that goal, giving multi-modality a clear discrete carrier:

    • MultiPath: predefine a set of trajectory anchors (obtained by clustering the training set); the network only outputs a probability + residual offset per anchor — turning regression into "classification + refinement," naturally multi-modal.
    • TNT (Target-driveN Trajectory): three stages — ① sample candidate goal points on the lanes and score them, ② regress a trajectory for each high-scoring goal, ③ score and de-duplicate the trajectory set (NMS). The benefit of goal conditioning is that goal points naturally lie on lanes and are physically feasible.
    • DenseTNT: removes the dependence on sparse anchors, directly predicts a probability heatmap over dense goal candidates, then uses a differentiable offline optimization to select the final goal set, avoiding the bias of hand-crafted anchors.

    4.4 Generative: Trajectron++ / CVAE

    Trajectron++ (Salzmann et al. 2020) uses a CVAE: it introduces a discrete latent variable \(z\) representing the "high-level intent mode," and the decoder generates a trajectory conditioned on \(z\). Sampling different \(z\) yields different modes, while elegantly fusing the map, agent heterogeneity (cars/pedestrians/cyclists), and dynamics constraints. The benefit of generative models is a natural sample distribution; the cost is that enough samples are needed to cover all modes.

    Figure 1 — Multi-modal trajectory prediction. The same car at an intersection has three mutually exclusive intents (straight / right turn / follow-and-slow), each with a probability $\pi$, and $\sum_k\pi_k=1$. The gray dashed line is the result of regressing a single trajectory (minimizing MSE) — it falls between the two peaks, ploughs off the curb, and is physically infeasible. This is exactly why "you must output multiple modes."

    5. Evaluation Metrics

    The core difficulty of prediction is "is the multi-modal set right, or is the single mode right," so metrics are almost all top-\(K\) in form — they let the model give \(K\) guesses and only penalize the best one, avoiding forcing the model to average over the two peaks.

    Let the ground-truth future be \(Y\) and the model output \(K\) candidates \(\{\hat Y^{(k)}\}\):

    • minADE\(_K\) (minimum Average Displacement Error): take the one of the \(K\) with the smallest average displacement error $$ \text{minADE}K = \min_h - y_h \big\rVert_2 . $$}\; \frac{1}{H}\sum_{h=1}^{H} \big\lVert \hat y^{(k)
    • minFDE\(_K\) (minimum Final Displacement Error): look only at the endpoint $$ \text{minFDE}K = \min_H - y_H \big\rVert_2 . $$}\; \big\lVert \hat y^{(k)
    • Miss Rate (MR): fraction of samples whose endpoint error exceeds a threshold (commonly \(2\,\text{m}\)) — measures "is at least one candidate reasonable."
    • brier-minFDE: \(\text{minFDE} + (1-\hat\pi^{(k^\*)})^2\), also penalizing how well the chosen mode's probability is calibrated (Argoverse's official ranking metric) — just "guessing right" is not enough, you must also get the probability right.

    Metric trap: looking at minADE\(_K\) alone encourages "casting a wide net" — outputting 6 divergent trajectories will always catch one, yet none has a credible probability. This is why Argoverse uses brier-minFDE and Waymo uses a probability-aware mAP (AP computed by ranking on confidence) as the headline metric.

    Metric What it measures Penalizes probability
    minADE\(_K\) full-horizon average error (best mode) no
    minFDE\(_K\) endpoint error (best mode) no
    Miss Rate whether a candidate is within threshold no
    brier-minFDE endpoint error + probability calibration yes
    mAP (Waymo) precision ranked by confidence yes

    6. Coupling with Planning

    6.1 Two Ailments of Naive Cascading

    The simplest approach is "predict once → treat the predicted trajectory as a fixed obstacle → plan around it." It has two structural defects:

    1. Ignores interaction (non-reactive): the predictor assumes other agents are unaffected by the ego. But in reality other agents react to the ego — once the ego cuts in, the other car slows down to yield. Treating other agents as "video replay" leads to two extremes: either over-conservatism (assuming they won't yield, so it waits forever) or misjudgment (planning with a prediction that ignored the ego's existence).
    2. The freezing robot problem: in crowded scenes, if every mode is treated as an inviolable obstacle, the free space is devoured by the "future cones" of all modes, the planner finds no feasible solution and stalls — even though a human driver would pass easily.

    6.2 Integrated Prediction-Planning & Game-Theoretic Interaction

    A more modern view treats prediction and planning as a single coupled, game-theoretic problem:

    • Conditional / ego-conditioned prediction: the predictor's input includes the ego's candidate plan, outputting \(p(\mathbf{Y}^{\text{other}} \mid \text{ego plan})\). The planner enumerates several of its own candidate plans and sees which induces the most favorable reaction from others — this models reactivity.
    • Joint prediction: directly output scene-consistent multi-agent joint trajectories (§2); the planner evaluates over the joint distribution.
    • Game-theoretic view: yielding at an intersection is essentially a multi-agent game, modelable with Nash/Stackelberg equilibria or hierarchical reasoning (level-\(k\)): "I bet you'll yield, so I go; you bet I'll go, so you yield."
    • Differentiable integration: UniAD / VAD put prediction and planning into the same differentiable network and back-propagate the final planning loss to shape prediction — prediction is no longer optimized for "prediction accuracy" but for "making planning drive well" (planning-oriented).

    In one sentence: the ultimate user of prediction is the planner, and the quality of prediction is ultimately measured by closed-loop driving quality, not offline minADE. A predictor with lower minADE but miscalibrated probabilities and no interaction modeling can make planning worse.

    Common Pitfalls

    1. Regressing a single trajectory with MSE: on a multi-modal distribution it collapses to the "fence-sitting mean," landing in an infeasible region (§4.1). Use a multi-modal loss (winner-takes-all / anchor classification / CVAE).
    2. Selecting models by minADE\(_K\) alone: this rewards "wide-net" guessing with untrustworthy probabilities. Use probability-calibrated metrics like brier-minFDE / mAP.
    3. Using marginal prediction as joint prediction: sampling each agent independently produces inconsistent combinations that clip through each other and fight for the same gap (§2).
    4. Pure trajectory extrapolation without the map: discards lane topology, the strongest prior, and is far worse than vectorized + lane graph.
    5. Cascading non-reactive prediction into planning: treating other-agent prediction as video replay causes over-conservatism or the freezing robot (§6).
    6. Inconsistent coordinate frames in training/inference: prediction often normalizes per agent ("self-centered + heading-aligned"); forgetting to transform back to the global frame during fusion/decoding introduces systematic bias.
    7. Deploying on the strength of offline metrics: the real test of prediction is closed-loop driving quality, not open-loop minADE.

    Frontier Trends

    1. Joint / scene-level prediction: moving from marginal to joint to guarantee multi-agent consistency (Waymo Sim Agents, Scene Transformer, MTR++).
    2. Planning-oriented integration: UniAD / VAD use the planning objective to shape prediction; see Planning & Decision Making.
    3. Occupancy-flow prediction: instead of discrete trajectories, predict spatio-temporal occupancy + flow fields, naturally handling arbitrary numbers of agents and uncertainty (Waymo Occupancy and Flow track).
    4. Transformer-based unified architectures: Wayformer, MTR use attention to uniformly fuse map and agents, express intent via learnable queries, and have nearly swept the major leaderboards.
    5. Large models / language entering prediction: using LLMs/VLMs for intent reasoning and interpretable prediction, injecting common sense like "it is waiting for a pedestrian, so it will stop."
    6. Closed-loop, reactive evaluation: shifting from open-loop minADE to closed-loop, interactive simulation evaluation (nuPlan, Waymo Sim Agents), forcing predictors to learn reactivity.
